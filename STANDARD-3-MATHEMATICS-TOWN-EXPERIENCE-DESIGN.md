# Standard 3 Mathematics Town: Experience Design

## Status

Proposed child-experience layer for review.

The proposed school-to-town flow, recurring cast, per-destination remediation, evidence summaries, and relationship arc are developed in `STANDARD-3-MATHEMATICS-CHARACTER-EXPERIENCE-INTEGRATION-BLUEPRINT.md`.

This document does not change the 18-stop educational architecture in `STANDARD-3-MATHEMATICS-TOWN-LEARNING-JOURNEY.md`. Stop order, curriculum objectives, prerequisites, demonstrations, guided practice, application evidence, and transfer requirements remain locked to that source.

This revision changes only how children experience those stops as destinations within the still-unnamed Standard 3 Mathematics town.

All destination names are working titles. They require child testing and Trinidad and Tobago cultural review before approval.

## Experience promise

Each map destination must answer:

1. **Why does this mathematics naturally exist here?**
2. **Would an eight-year-old genuinely want to visit?**

The child should see places, people, movement, mysteries, materials, and community needs. The formal curriculum remains available to parents but does not become the map language.

## Destination design rules

1. Name the place or experience, not the academic function.
2. Give the child a useful role, not a worksheet assignment.
3. Let the resident use Mathematics because their real activity requires it.
4. Show a visible outcome: books returned, parcels delivered, a kite flying, a playground opened, or a radio report shared.
5. Keep the Caribbean identity lived-in and varied across home, work, school, transport, nature, recreation, commerce, and community life.
6. Avoid turning every stop into a festival, market, food activity, or school exercise.
7. Do not depend on one cultural symbol, food, sport, or family structure.
8. Keep the stop age-appropriate for Standards 3-5, with warmth and adventure rather than preschool fantasy.
9. Preserve the teaching sequence beneath the experience: encounter, meaning, demonstration, guided use, varied practice, application, explanation, and transfer.
10. Working names may change. The mathematical reason for the destination may not.

# Destination experiences

## Stop 1 - The Grand Library Book Hunt

**Locked curriculum role:** Counting, number names, numerals, sequencing, and estimating collections to 10,000.

### Why the mathematics naturally exists here

A busy community library must count returned books, organise collections, estimate how many books are waiting in unsorted carts, label shelves, and reconcile a missing section before reopening. Accurate quantities matter because books must reach the correct shelves and readers.

### Why a child would want to visit

The destination offers exploration rather than administration: tall shelves, rolling book carts, reading corners, a return chute, and a search for books that have reached the wrong collection. The child gets to uncover and restore the library rather than complete a register.

### Natural mentor

A lively community librarian or older library assistant who knows the collection and models organising before counting.

### Experience arc

- **Encounter:** Returned books and supply boxes have been mixed across the library.
- **Interactive demonstration:** A book cart is grouped into tens and hundreds while its numeral and number name update.
- **Guided experience:** The child estimates a cart, groups it, labels collections, and places quantity records in order.
- **Reusable play:** Search shelves for mismatched labels; race the return chute without a strict timer; estimate, organise, verify.
- **Application mission:** Restore several library sections and locate the one book collection whose written record cannot match what is present.

### Visible outcome

Shelves fill correctly, reading spaces reopen, and previously missing books become visible in their proper sections.

### Authenticity guardrail

Ground the destination in a recognisable Trinidad and Tobago community library rather than a generic magical castle library. Wonder comes from discovery, height, movement, and restored access to stories.

## Stop 2 - The Harbour Storehouse

**Locked curriculum role:** Place value, expanded notation, digit value, comparison, ordering, and rounding.

### Why the mathematics naturally exists here

Crates and supplies arrive in large quantities. Workers must interpret four-digit records, compare shipments, order unloading priorities, and use rounded totals for quick storage planning while preserving exact counts for the ledger.

### Why a child would want to visit

The harbour offers cranes, carts, stacked crates, painted numbers, arriving boats, and the satisfying movement of supplies into the correct bays. The child helps direct a working place where decisions visibly change the scene.

### Natural mentor

A storehouse keeper, wharf coordinator, or inventory lead who moves comfortably between exact and approximate quantities.

### Experience arc

- **Encounter:** Several arriving shipments have damaged or inconsistent labels.
- **Interactive demonstration:** Crates regroup across ones, tens, hundreds, and thousands without changing the total.
- **Guided experience:** The child rebuilds expanded records, compares shipments place by place, orders unloading, and rounds for storage estimates.
- **Reusable play:** Repair labels; arrange digit plates under constraints; guide crates into correctly numbered bays.
- **Application mission:** Produce an exact unloading order and a rounded storage plan, then resolve one place-value entry that would send a shipment to the wrong bay.

### Visible outcome

The crowded wharf clears, crates stack safely, and outgoing community deliveries become available.

### Authenticity guardrail

This destination depends on the eventual town geography supporting a harbour. If it does not, preserve the supply-arrival experience in a central warehouse or transport depot rather than forcing a coastline.

## Stop 3 - The Kite-Maker's Yard

**Locked curriculum role:** Ascending, descending, repeating, and growing number patterns; rules; missing elements; multiplication/division facts; error recognition.

### Why the mathematics naturally exists here

Kites and their tails use repeated pieces, increasing arrangements, measured sequences, and predictable material counts. A kite-maker must know the rule to reproduce a design and calculate what later sections require.

### Why a child would want to visit

Children can choose colours, assemble tails, test patterns in the breeze, find the one faulty section, and finally see completed kites rise. The destination combines making with an immediate, joyful result.

### Natural mentor

A kite-maker, craft worker, or older youth who builds and repairs kites and explains patterns through materials.

### Experience arc

- **Encounter:** Several kite tails and decorative panels are incomplete or built under the wrong rule.
- **Interactive demonstration:** A pattern grows one stage at a time while its number rule and required materials remain connected.
- **Guided experience:** The child completes sequences, identifies mistakes, states rules, and predicts later material needs.
- **Reusable play:** Pattern repair; match design to rule; build the fifth element; create a valid alternative pattern.
- **Application mission:** Finish a set of kites using limited pieces, correct one broken rule, and prepare the materials required for the next two designs.

### Visible outcome

Completed kites appear above the yard, with patterns the learner helped create.

### Authenticity guardrail

Treat kite-making as an ordinary regional hobby and skilled craft, not a decorative tropical symbol. Local review should shape materials, construction, and seasonal references.

## Stop 4 - The Parcel Post Mystery

**Locked curriculum role:** Equality, unknowns on either side of the equals sign, inverse operations, missing quantities, checking, and error explanation.

### Why the mathematics naturally exists here

Parcels enter and leave the post office, but dispatch records, shelf counts, and weighing records must balance. Missing information can be recovered from what arrived, what left, and what remains.

### Why a child would want to visit

This stop is a gentle mystery. Parcels travel along rollers, stamps and route labels offer clues, scales react, and one misplaced parcel explains why the records do not balance.

### Natural mentor

A postal worker, parcel clerk, or route sorter who reconciles records and uses inverse checks.

### Experience arc

- **Encounter:** The closing parcel count does not match the delivery record.
- **Interactive demonstration:** Physical parcel groups and number sentences balance together as quantities enter or leave.
- **Guided experience:** The child restores missing values in different positions and checks them with inverse operations.
- **Reusable play:** Balance the shelves; recover missing stock; repair a broken number sentence; inspect incorrect working.
- **Application mission:** Trace several parcel movements, restore incomplete records, and identify the first incorrect calculation causing the mismatch.

### Visible outcome

The missing parcel reaches the correct route, the dispatch board balances, and the post office can close successfully.

### Authenticity guardrail

Keep the mystery focused on problem solving, not suspicion of a person. Errors come from mixed labels, incomplete records, or ordinary mistakes.

## Stop 5 - The Corner Shop Challenge

**Locked curriculum role:** Addition, subtraction, bills, multi-step money problems, best buy, profit/loss, estimation, and inverse checking.

### Why the mathematics naturally exists here

A neighbourhood shop combines prices, quantities, bills, stock, change, and purchasing decisions. The child must calculate and compare because a real list and limited budget must be satisfied.

### Why a child would want to visit

The shelves are full of recognisable household and school items, the order changes based on choices, and the child controls the basket. Several valid combinations create agency rather than one predetermined answer.

### Natural mentor

A shopkeeper or family member planning a purchase who estimates first and checks each bill.

### Experience arc

- **Encounter:** A supply list must be completed without exceeding the available amount.
- **Interactive demonstration:** Prices combine on an open number line and money model, with an estimate remaining visible beside the exact total.
- **Guided experience:** The child completes bill entries, finds differences, works backward from totals, and compares combinations.
- **Reusable play:** Receipt repair; basket builder; missing price; profit-or-loss sort; choose under budget.
- **Application mission:** Complete a multi-item purchase, recover one missing bill value, and explain which valid combination best meets the need.

### Visible outcome

The correct supplies are packed, the bill reconciles, and the shop's stock display updates.

### Authenticity guardrail

Use ordinary local shopping without presenting financial hardship as entertainment. The challenge is thoughtful choice and accuracy, not anxiety about scarcity.

## Stop 6 - The Busy Delivery Depot

**Locked curriculum role:** Multiplication, division, arrays, distributive reasoning, rates, unitary method, remainders, mental strategies, and inverse checking.

### Why the mathematics naturally exists here

Goods must be packed into equal groups, assigned to vehicles or trips, and delivered at known rates. Remainders have practical consequences: another package, another trip, a shared leftover, or unused stock.

### Why a child would want to visit

The depot is active: parcels roll, carts fill, route tokens move, and vehicles depart as each plan succeeds. Children can test different arrangements and see which plan works.

### Natural mentor

A delivery coordinator, depot worker, or driver who thinks in groups, loads, trips, and rates.

### Experience arc

- **Encounter:** Several community deliveries need efficient packing and routing.
- **Interactive demonstration:** The same goods transform among arrays, packages, multiplication, and division records.
- **Guided experience:** The child decomposes factors, fills packages, calculates repeated rates, and interprets remainders.
- **Reusable play:** Packing puzzle; array builder; delivery-rate table; remainder decision; division error inspection.
- **Application mission:** Prepare multiple deliveries, calculate packages and trips, and adapt when one vehicle's capacity changes.

### Visible outcome

Loaded vehicles leave for visible destinations and empty bays show that every valid delivery has departed.

### Authenticity guardrail

Use locally plausible vans, carts, bicycles, or other delivery methods according to the final setting. Avoid turning transport into a generic North American logistics warehouse.

## Stop 7 - The Big Bakehouse

**Locked curriculum role:** Fraction meaning through equal parts, area/set/linear models, numerator/denominator, words/symbols, and early equivalence.

### Why the mathematics naturally exists here

Food and ingredients must be divided fairly, trays partitioned evenly, fruit or baked items grouped, and measured strips or portions recorded. Fairness makes equal parts necessary.

### Why a child would want to visit

Children help prepare and arrange recognisable foods, watch trays change as portions are shared, and solve a fairness problem with a visible result. The bakehouse feels active and sensory without requiring audio.

### Natural mentor

A baker, cook, food preparer, or family/community volunteer who values fair sharing and careful preparation.

### Experience arc

- **Encounter:** Several trays and ingredient sets must be shared among changing groups.
- **Interactive demonstration:** The same fraction moves among a tray, a set of fruit or baked items, and a measured strip.
- **Guided experience:** The child creates equal parts, labels fractions, matches representations, and repairs unequal sharing plans.
- **Reusable play:** Fair-share builder; fraction model matching; reconstruct the whole; find the unequal partition.
- **Application mission:** Prepare fair shares for several groups and provide matching area, set, and linear records for each amount.

### Visible outcome

Prepared trays fill the serving area and every group receives a visibly fair share.

### Authenticity guardrail

Use varied local foods and non-food models. No single pizza, cake, or roti image should carry the entire fraction concept.

## Stop 8 - The Tailor's Loft

**Locked curriculum role:** Equivalent fractions, comparison/order, number lines, proper/improper/mixed forms, and same-denominator addition/subtraction.

### Why the mathematics naturally exists here

Fabric, ribbon, cord, and pattern pieces come in fractional lengths. A tailor combines pieces, substitutes equivalent lengths, compares what remains, and works beyond one whole unit.

### Why a child would want to visit

The loft is full of colour, rolling fabric, measuring tables, pattern pieces, and choices. The child assembles materials into something that will be used later rather than manipulating anonymous strips.

### Natural mentor

A tailor, seamstress, costume maker, or fabric craft worker who measures and combines material precisely.

### Experience arc

- **Encounter:** Required material lengths must be assembled from the pieces available.
- **Interactive demonstration:** Fractional fabric strips subdivide into equivalent pieces without changing length and cross the one-whole boundary.
- **Guided experience:** The child builds equivalents, orders lengths, uses number lines, converts mixed/improper forms, and combines like-sized parts.
- **Reusable play:** Fabric exchange; cover the same length; mixed-number builder; order the offcuts; meet the material target.
- **Application mission:** Prepare materials for several orders, substitute equivalent pieces, and combine parts into amounts greater than one whole.

### Visible outcome

Completed fabric bundles and pattern pieces move to later makers, showing that the measured work matters beyond this stop.

### Authenticity guardrail

Represent tailoring as skilled community work. Clothing and fabric references should span everyday, school, formal, and celebratory use rather than defaulting entirely to Carnival.

## Stop 9 - The Builders' Yard

**Locked curriculum role:** Solids, plane shapes, polygons, properties, orientation, faces, edges, vertices, frames, sketches, and construction.

### Why the mathematics naturally exists here

Builders choose shapes and solids because their properties determine whether an object can stack, roll, enclose, support, or fit. Frames require the correct number and type of components.

### Why a child would want to visit

Children manipulate beams, panels, blocks, signs, and package forms, test structures, and repair something that cannot stand or close. The destination encourages experimentation.

### Natural mentor

A carpenter, builder, mason, repairer, package maker, or skilled craft worker.

### Experience arc

- **Encounter:** Several objects and structures are unfinished or use unsuitable forms.
- **Interactive demonstration:** Solids rotate, become transparent, reveal faces, and reduce to edge frames; polygons remain themselves when turned.
- **Guided experience:** The child classifies by property, counts components, sketches, builds frames, and selects forms for purposes.
- **Reusable play:** Property detective; frame builder; rotated-shape challenge; stack/roll/fit test; repair the structure.
- **Application mission:** Construct or repair a set of useful objects while meeting stated shape and component constraints.

### Visible outcome

Working shelves, containers, signs, or frames appear around the town and remain visible in later destinations.

### Authenticity guardrail

Use materials and building forms plausible to the fictional Caribbean setting. Avoid copying one real architectural tradition as the universal local style.

## Stop 10 - The Mas Camp

**Locked curriculum role:** Symmetry, lines of symmetry, completing/creating symmetrical forms, geometry language, and visual pattern transfer.

### Why the mathematics naturally exists here

Costume sections, headpieces, bands, borders, and decorative arrangements may require reflected parts, balanced placement, repeated rules, and exact component counts.

### Why a child would want to visit

This is one of the journey's expressive destinations: colour, movement, large forms, changing materials, and the chance to complete a design that later joins a procession or display.

### Natural mentor

A mas designer, wire bender, costume maker, band craft worker, or visual artist with locally reviewed knowledge.

### Experience arc

- **Encounter:** Several sections are almost complete, but mirrored pieces and patterned borders do not align.
- **Interactive demonstration:** A fold or mirror reveals corresponding points and lines of symmetry.
- **Guided experience:** The child reflects grid points, tests shapes, repairs near-symmetry, and extends pattern rules.
- **Reusable play:** Mirror match; missing costume section; symmetry-line search; pattern-border builder; design repair.
- **Application mission:** Complete a balanced costume or display section under material limits and explain the correction to one subtle error.

### Visible outcome

The completed section joins a larger display or procession, making the learner's design visible beyond the workshop.

### Authenticity guardrail

This stop requires Trinidad and Tobago cultural practitioners. It must represent skilled mas-making and community creativity, not use feathers, glitter, or Carnival imagery as generic Caribbean decoration.

## Stop 11 - The Maxi Route Adventure

**Locked curriculum role:** Kilometres, metres, centimetres, unit choice, estimation, measurement, conversion, repeated intervals, and distance problems.

### Why the mathematics naturally exists here

Routes, stops, signs, walking connections, and delivery distances require different units. Equal spacing creates interval reasoning, while route choices require estimation and comparison.

### Why a child would want to visit

The learner moves across the town, reveals new views, places stop markers, and helps a maxi reach its destinations. It gives the journey momentum after several workshop-based stops.

### Natural mentor

A maxi driver, route dispatcher, transport organiser, or experienced conductor presented with local review.

### Experience arc

- **Encounter:** A changed route needs new distance records and correctly spaced stops.
- **Interactive demonstration:** Centimetres, metres, and kilometres appear at different zoom scales; markers and gaps are counted separately.
- **Guided experience:** The child estimates, selects units, measures, converts, places stops, and compares routes.
- **Reusable play:** Unit chooser; stop-spacing puzzle; first-to-last distance; route comparison; measure and convert.
- **Application mission:** Prepare a valid route and revise it after one stop or road condition changes.

### Visible outcome

The maxi travels the corrected route, passengers or deliveries reach their stops, and new parts of the town become connected.

### Authenticity guardrail

The maxi experience must reflect real Trinidad and Tobago transport patterns, terminology, and visual cues without teaching unsafe road behaviour or caricaturing drivers and passengers.

## Stop 12 - The Playground Project

**Locked curriculum role:** Perimeter, area, square units, conservation, regular/irregular shapes, unit choice, and the distinction between boundary and surface.

### Why the mathematics naturally exists here

A playground or recreation space needs safe boundaries, usable activity areas, paths, garden sections, and surfaces. Fencing requires perimeter; ground covering and activity zones require area.

### Why a child would want to visit

Children help shape a place intended for them. They can try multiple layouts, choose among activity zones, and watch an empty or worn space become usable.

### Natural mentor

A groundskeeper, builder, landscape worker, coach, or school/community project organiser.

### Experience arc

- **Encounter:** An open space must be redesigned within boundary, surface, and material limits.
- **Interactive demonstration:** Grid shapes rearrange while area remains constant and perimeter changes.
- **Guided experience:** The child traces boundaries, covers surfaces, builds specified areas, compares designs, and chooses units.
- **Reusable play:** Fence versus surface sort; grid builder; equal-area challenge; material estimator; fit the play zones.
- **Application mission:** Produce a workable playground plan and revise it when one dimension or material allowance changes.

### Visible outcome

The finished play space opens and later characters can be seen using the learner's layout.

### Authenticity guardrail

Design for varied play, accessibility, shade, drainage, and community use rather than importing a generic suburban playground.

## Stop 13 - The Farmers' Market

**Locked curriculum role:** Grams, kilograms, scale reading, mass estimation, comparison, ordering, conversion, equality, repeated units, and contextual money.

### Why the mathematics naturally exists here

Produce and packaged goods are weighed, compared, labelled, combined, and priced. Visual size can be misleading, so the scale and unit matter.

### Why a child would want to visit

The market is busy and choice-rich. Baskets fill, scales move, produce arrives from growers, and correct packages travel to real customers or community kitchens.

### Natural mentor

A farmer, produce vendor, market organiser, or cooperative grower.

### Experience arc

- **Encounter:** Orders need exact masses, but several labels and packages do not match.
- **Interactive demonstration:** Dial and balance scales connect gram/kilogram records while differently sized objects reveal mass misconceptions.
- **Guided experience:** The child estimates, weighs, converts, assembles targets, orders packages, and balances repeated masses.
- **Reusable play:** Estimate then weigh; target-mass builder; label repair; balance challenge; compare mass and value.
- **Application mission:** Prepare several produce orders and resolve a mismatch between a label, a scale, and the required total.

### Visible outcome

Correctly weighed baskets move to homes, shops, or the bakehouse, linking this stop to earlier and later places.

### Authenticity guardrail

Use locally plausible produce, packaging, weighing practices, and market activity without reducing Caribbean life to colourful fruit stalls.

## Stop 14 - Water Park Adventure

**Locked curriculum role:** Millilitres, litres, halves/quarters/multiples, capacity estimation, comparison, conversion, division, and resource allocation.

### Why the mathematics naturally exists here

A community water park must prepare splash tanks, fountains, refill stations, and water-play equipment before opening. Each feature needs a particular quantity of water, differently shaped containers can hold the same capacity, and the operations team must measure accurately so every attraction works safely.

### Why a child would want to visit

The child gets an early-access adventure through water channels, tipping buckets, fountains, spray arches, and splash zones. Every correct capacity decision activates another part of the park, creating an immediate and playful reason to keep going.

### Natural mentor

A friendly lifeguard or water-park operations technician who understands both safe water play and how the park's systems work.

### Experience arc

- **Encounter:** The park cannot open because several attractions have incomplete or mismatched capacity instructions.
- **Interactive demonstration:** Water moves between differently shaped transparent containers while litre, millilitre, half, and quarter records remain linked.
- **Guided experience:** The child estimates capacity, fills marked targets, converts units, composes litres, and determines how many complete smaller containers are needed.
- **Reusable play:** Fill and compare; one-litre builder; tipping-bucket balance; container substitution; repair the faded capacity label.
- **Application mission:** Prepare several attractions for opening, replace an unavailable container with an equivalent combination, and distribute a fixed supply without overfilling or leaving a required feature short.

### Visible outcome

Fountains begin, spray arches activate, the tipping bucket releases, and families enter the completed water-play area.

### Authenticity guardrail

Design it as a believable Caribbean community recreation space with shade, family areas, visible safety practices, and responsible water use—not a luxury resort or generic tropical fantasy. The child supports a supervised opening check and is never asked to make real safety decisions. Avoid speed-based tasks around water.

## Stop 15 - Sports Day at the Savannah

**Locked curriculum role:** Analogue/digital time, five-minute intervals, a.m./p.m., 24-hour time, duration, calendars, date formats, fractions of an hour, and schedule revision.

### Why the mathematics naturally exists here

Races, matches, breaks, arrivals, equipment setup, and presentations must occur in sequence and for known durations. Delays create genuine schedule problems.

### Why a child would want to visit

The destination offers movement, choice, multiple sports and activities, a large open setting, and the excitement of helping the whole day run. The child is not required to be the athlete to have an important role.

### Natural mentor

A coach, sports organiser, timekeeper, groundskeeper, or older student helper.

### Experience arc

- **Encounter:** The day's timetable contains conflicts and one event has been delayed.
- **Interactive demonstration:** Analogue, digital, 24-hour, timeline, and calendar representations move together.
- **Guided experience:** The child reads times, calculates duration, works backward, sequences events, and repairs conflicts.
- **Reusable play:** Clock match; event scheduler; duration trail; calendar finder; reschedule after delay.
- **Application mission:** Coordinate the complete activity timetable and revise it after a weather or equipment delay without shortening required events.

### Visible outcome

Activities begin on time, participants reach the correct spaces, and the closing presentation happens successfully.

### Authenticity guardrail

Include cricket, football, netball, athletics, and non-competitive activities without assuming one sport or ability defines every child's interest. Avoid public ranking mechanics.

## Stop 16 - The Mangrove Count

**Locked curriculum role:** Formulating real questions, selecting relevant data, collecting/classifying observations, tallying, totalling frequencies, and identifying data-quality problems.

### Why the mathematics naturally exists here

An environmental group wants to understand what is present along a mangrove trail and when. Reliable observations require a clear question, consistent categories, accurate tallies, and awareness of duplicate or missing records.

### Why a child would want to visit

This stop is an expedition. The learner explores a new environment, spots changing wildlife or plant evidence, records discoveries, and contributes to a real decision rather than receiving a finished table.

### Natural mentor

An environmental guide, ranger, science-club coordinator, or community conservation volunteer.

### Experience arc

- **Encounter:** The group has observations but cannot answer its question because the categories and records are inconsistent.
- **Interactive demonstration:** Observations move into categories and grouped tallies; changing a category reveals how the result changes.
- **Guided experience:** The child chooses an answerable question, classifies sightings or observations, tallies, totals, and repairs data problems.
- **Reusable play:** Observation sort; rapid tally; category check; duplicate detector; missing-data finder.
- **Application mission:** Complete a small field investigation and produce a verified frequency record for a later community report.

### Visible outcome

The trail record becomes complete, observation markers appear on a field map, and the evidence is sent onward for interpretation.

### Authenticity guardrail

Environmental content requires local ecological review. Do not invent wildlife, encourage unsafe interaction, or turn a mangrove into an exotic adventure backdrop detached from conservation.

## Stop 17 - The Community Radio Station

**Locked curriculum role:** Bar graphs, axes, categories, scales, representation transformation, comparison, combined values, thresholds, decisions, and communication.

### Why the mathematics naturally exists here

A radio station or community information service turns survey and observation data into understandable reports. The scale, labels, calculations, and conclusion must be accurate before information is shared.

### Why a child would want to visit

The learner enters a control room, prepares displays, checks incoming community reports, and decides what can responsibly be announced. The result is communicated beyond the screen.

### Natural mentor

A radio host, community reporter, producer, librarian, or information officer who distinguishes evidence from unsupported claims.

### Experience arc

- **Encounter:** Several reports contain incomplete graphs or conclusions that do not match the data.
- **Interactive demonstration:** The same frequency table appears under different valid scales, while missing labels or misleading intervals reveal representation problems.
- **Guided experience:** The child constructs graphs, transforms tallies/pictographs, compares categories, combines values, and calculates threshold changes.
- **Reusable play:** Scale selector; graph builder; misleading-graph detector; combine categories; change the outcome; evidence-or-opinion sort.
- **Application mission:** Prepare an accurate community report from the Mangrove Count and other town records, then recommend an action supported by the evidence.

### Visible outcome

The completed report appears on community boards or radios in earlier destinations, making the learner's evidence travel through the town.

### Authenticity guardrail

Treat local radio and community information as skilled public service. Do not imitate a specific broadcaster or use exaggerated accents, personalities, or sensational news framing.

## Stop 18 - The Grand Community Fair

**Locked curriculum role:** Independent integration across Number, Geometry, Measurement, and Statistics using accumulated evidence.

### Why the mathematics naturally exists here

A community fair or family day brings together attendance estimates, supplies, prices, grouping, measured spaces, structures, schedules, transport, and evidence-based decisions. The mathematics is necessary because the event must actually work under changing conditions.

### Why a child would want to visit

This is the payoff destination. Places and residents from across the journey contribute, earlier environmental improvements reappear, and the child sees the town come together because of work completed along the path.

### Natural mentor

A community coordinator who provides the need and available information but does not tell the learner which mathematics to use. Earlier mentors contribute records and constraints.

### Experience arc

- **Encounter:** The fair plan is incomplete and one condition will change after the initial plan is created.
- **Interactive demonstration:** No new concept is taught; a planning surface helps organise knowns, unknowns, tools, estimates, and checks.
- **Guided experience:** Support is strategic only: identify the goal, choose information, represent, estimate, solve, check, and revise.
- **Reusable play:** Compose proven mechanics from the full journey rather than introduce a new game.
- **Application mission:** Interpret attendance data, determine quantities, compare a purchase or allocation, design a usable space, coordinate a schedule or delivery, adapt to a change, and justify key decisions.

### Visible outcome

The fair opens across the central community space. Books, kites, deliveries, food, designs, produce, measured refreshment and water-play supplies, sports activities, environmental findings, and radio reports provide visual continuity with the journey.

### Authenticity guardrail

The final event must be developed with local reviewers. It should feel like an ordinary community gathering with broad participation, not a generic tropical festival or a compressed display of every cultural reference.

# Experience coherence across the path

## Emotional rhythm

The destination sequence deliberately changes activity type:

- explore and organise;
- observe a working harbour;
- make and fly;
- solve a mystery;
- shop and choose;
- pack and dispatch;
- prepare and share;
- design with fabric;
- build;
- create expressive forms;
- travel;
- improve a play space;
- weigh market goods;
- manage a water system;
- coordinate sports;
- explore nature;
- report to the community;
- bring the town together.

This variation prevents the path from feeling like eighteen versions of a shop, classroom, or workstation.

## Mentor balance

Mentors arise from real roles: librarian, wharf coordinator, kite-maker, postal worker, shopkeeper, delivery organiser, baker, tailor, builder, mas practitioner, maxi driver, groundskeeper, grower/vendor, water-park operations worker, coach, environmental guide, radio producer, and community coordinator.

The later cast should combine some functions into recurring residents so the town feels socially connected rather than populated by eighteen one-time instructors. Character consolidation happens after destination approval and must not change the mathematical reason each mentor belongs.

## Magic without unnecessary complexity

The premium feeling should come from consequence and continuity rather than fantasy effects:

- repaired places remain repaired;
- items prepared in one destination appear in another;
- the maxi route connects previously visited places;
- library books, produce, fabric, signs, measured supplies, and radio reports travel through the town;
- completed kites, costumes, playgrounds, water-park features, and fair spaces visibly improve;
- final scenes contain evidence of earlier work.

This makes the town feel alive while allowing the underlying interactions and assets to remain reusable.

# Risks and revisions to test

1. **Harbour dependency:** If the final town is inland, replace the Harbour Storehouse with an equally active central warehouse or transport arrival point.
2. **Too many maker destinations:** Stops 3, 8, 9, and 10 are mathematically distinct but visually related. Their materials, scale, environment, and activity must be clearly differentiated.
3. **Commerce repetition:** The Corner Shop and Farmers' Market must feel different: value decisions at the shop, mass and producer activity at the market.
4. **Event dependence:** Sports Day and the Community Fair should not make the whole journey feel like preparation for one celebration unless that larger story is explicitly approved later.
5. **Cultural compression:** Mas, maxi transport, market life, and mangroves must not become a checklist of Trinidadian symbols. Each exists because the mathematics is authentic there.
6. **Urban/rural balance:** The destination set mixes harbour, shop, transport, radio, recreation, market, and nature, but local reviewers should test whether the combination feels like one plausible fictional community.
7. **Production scale:** Eighteen map destinations do not require eighteen unrelated art systems. Reuse residents, materials, routes, environmental props, and visible consequences without making places feel duplicated.

# Decisions required before visual world design

1. Validate each working destination with a qualified local educator: does the Mathematics still arise naturally and completely?
2. Test destination comprehension and excitement with Standards 3-5 children.
3. Review the full set for cultural authenticity and contextual balance.
4. Decide whether the Harbour Storehouse fits the eventual geography.
5. Decide whether the Mas Camp, Maxi Route, Mangrove Count, and Community Fair are appropriate as persistent destinations or event experiences.
6. Consolidate mentor functions into a recurring cast only after the destinations are approved.
7. Develop final names only after geography and the country-wide naming system are known.
