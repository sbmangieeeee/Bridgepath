## Curriculum coverage

The supplied guide contains four major Mathematics strands:

1. **Number:** number concepts to 10,000; place value and rounding; patterns and relationships; four operations; mental mathematics; fractions.
2. **Geometry:** solids; plane shapes and polygons; symmetry; mathematical language.
3. **Measurement:** length, perimeter, mass, time, capacity, area, units and conversions.
4. **Statistics:** real-life investigations; tally charts; bar graphs; scales; interpretation; justified decisions.

The curriculum also expects conceptual understanding, procedural fluency, strategic competence, adaptive reasoning, and a productive disposition toward mathematics. These are evidence dimensions across the whole town, not isolated chapters.

## First vertical slice: Market Day money and change

The current vertical slice remains useful because it demonstrates the full Bridgepath learning loop in a familiar setting. It should be treated as one curriculum-aligned adventure, not as proof that the full Standard 3 town has been mapped or completed.

### Proposed learning stops

1. **Auntie Anisa's Price Board** — connect dollar and cent values to prices and quantities.
2. **Build the Payment** — compose exact amounts using visible notes and coins.
3. **Check the Total** — add purchases and estimate whether the total is reasonable.
4. **Choose the Better Buy** — compare options using price and quantity.
5. **Check the Change** — use subtraction and inverse checking rather than accepting the displayed answer.
6. **Event Supply Run** — complete several purchases within a budget and respond to one changed price or quantity.

Before implementation, these stops must be mapped to the exact Number objective IDs and prerequisites in the curriculum guide. If money notation or change is not itself an explicit Standard 3 objective, it remains a valid application context only when the underlying approved objectives are stated precisely.

## How one concept becomes an adventure

### Example: comparing values

- **Community need:** Two delivery crates have different stock counts, and the fuller crate must go to the busier stall.
- **Meaning:** The learner builds both quantities with place-value materials and compares thousands, then hundreds, tens, and units.
- **Memory cue:** A hungry animal or hinged sign opens toward the greater quantity.
- **Symbol connection:** The cue is linked explicitly to `>`, `<`, and `=` and to the words greater than, less than, and equal to.
- **Cue fading:** The animal disappears; the child compares numerals and explains which place determined the result.
- **Application:** The child selects the correct crate from an unfamiliar stock list and justifies the decision.
- **Transfer:** Comparison returns later in best-buy, measurement, fraction, and graph tasks.

### Example: fractions

- **Community need:** The bakery must share trays fairly and adjust quantities for different groups.
- **Meaning:** Equal parts are established before fraction names are introduced.
- **Representations:** Cake or roti portions provide an area model; baskets of fruit provide a set model; a delivery route provides a linear/number-line model.
- **Connection:** Numerator and denominator language is linked across all three representations.
- **Variation:** Learners identify equivalents, compare unlike denominators through models, and work with proper, improper, and mixed forms.
- **Application:** The learner changes the sharing plan when more residents arrive and explains why the new portions are fair.
- **Transfer:** Fractions return in capacity, time, measurement, and later event planning.

## Recurring resident roles

Characters should emerge from the curriculum applications rather than being assigned as generic lesson mascots.

| Working role | Natural mathematical territory | Teaching personality opportunity |
|---|---|---|
| Produce vendor / market organiser | Money, operations, estimation, best buy, profit and loss | Thinks aloud and always checks whether an answer is sensible |
| Depot keeper | Counting, place value, comparison, grouping | Organised, practical, uses objects before symbols |
| Baker or cook | Fractions, mass, capacity, time | Demonstrates slowly, values fairness and precision |
| Tailor / sign painter | Patterns, symmetry, length, area | Visual thinker who sketches and tests ideas |
| Builder / carpenter | Geometry, perimeter, area, measurement | Connects diagrams to real structures and notices unit mistakes |
| Delivery coordinator | Multiplication, division, rates, time, distance | Tries efficient strategies and compares routes |
| Community reporter / librarian | Statistics, vocabulary, explanation | Asks what the evidence shows and whether a conclusion is justified |

Final characters need names, histories, relationships, visual silhouettes, dialogue boundaries, and local cultural review. One resident may support multiple chapters so Market Village feels like a community rather than a sequence of presenters.
