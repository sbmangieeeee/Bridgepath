# Bridgepath Product Discovery Brief

Status: DRAFT FOR APPROVAL  
Date: 14 July 2026  
Product stage: Pre-pilot discovery  

## Product decision

Bridgepath will be a parent-first curriculum mastery adventure for children in Standards 3–5. Children explore Kairana, a fictional Caribbean country with six towns. Arouca Grove is the current playable Standard 3 Mathematics town and contains all 18 curriculum-derived stops. The remaining towns are reserved for future learning journeys and have no approved curriculum assignments yet.

The first validated release will be the complete 18-stop Standard 3 Mathematics journey inside Arouca Grove. Standards 4–5 and English remain part of the product vision, but their future town assignments are not yet approved.

## Problem

Parents usually receive broad, class-relative information at the end of a school term. That information arrives too late and does not show which national curriculum concepts a child understands, can apply independently, or needs help with.

By Standard 5, many families add frequent after-school lessons to prepare for SEA. The risk is that intervention begins after gaps have accumulated and becomes focused on answering examination questions rather than developing transferable understanding.

Bridgepath should answer two parent questions early:

1. What should my child understand at this point in the curriculum?
2. What should my child and I do next about a specific gap?

## Evidence and what remains unproven

There is evidence of the underlying problem and existing spending:

- The 2025 SEA national mean was 52.1% in Mathematics; 11.65% of candidates scored 30% or below across the assessment. [Ministry of Education SEA 2025 statistics](https://storage.moe.gov.tt/wpdevelopment/2025/07/Media-Release-2025-SEA-Recognition-Ceremony-A-Celebration-of-Achievement-Equity-and-New-Benchmarks-in-Education.pdf)
- Families already pay for recurring lessons and exam preparation. Local tutoring services explicitly sell assessment, personal learning plans, continuous monitoring, and SEA outcomes. [PTI tutoring](https://ptitutoring.com/)
- Pennacool already provides local curriculum practice, revision, worked examples, reports, and rewards. Bridgepath therefore cannot differentiate through localisation or gamification alone. [Pennacool](https://sea.pennacool.com/pennacool/pennacool_aboutus.php)
- The Ministry curriculum itself prioritises assessment for learning and differentiated instruction, which supports earlier, objective-level feedback rather than term-end reporting alone. [Ministry primary curriculum](https://www.moe.gov.tt/primary/)

What remains unproven is demand for Bridgepath specifically. No parent has yet committed money, regular usage, or a child’s learning time. The pilot must test whether parents trust the evidence and whether children voluntarily return to complete meaningful learning.

## Primary users

### Child

A Standard 3–5 learner who needs concepts taught clearly, practised with support, and applied in unfamiliar worded or real-life situations.

### Parent

The first purchaser and account owner. The parent wants early warning, plain-language curriculum visibility, and a realistic action they can take without becoming a teacher.

### Deferred user

Schools are a later channel, not a pilot user. Teacher classes, assignments, rostering, school reporting, procurement, and institutional consent would add a second product before the parent-child learning loop is validated.

## Product promise

**Know what your child understands, help them close the gap, and watch them use it in the adventure.**

Bridgepath is not initially a replacement for school, a complete virtual school, or an SEA revision bank. It supplements school by detecting and closing curriculum gaps before late-stage examination preparation.

## World and curriculum structure

The approved hierarchy is:

`Fictional Caribbean country → curriculum town → unit location → concept mission path → learning activity node`

Each curriculum town represents one pairing, such as Standard 3 Mathematics or Standard 4 English. The country overview shows a larger future world. Appropriate towns are open; future towns may be visible as previews but must not imply that unavailable curriculum content is complete.

Inside a town:

- A market, library, garden, workshop, recreation ground, or other community location represents a curriculum unit or coherent concept group.
- A mission path shows a clear next step and visible progress.
- Different adventures may teach or apply related objectives, but curriculum prerequisites control progression.
- The child chooses meaningful adventures within the available curriculum. The child does not choose their own Standard or bypass required evidence.

## Learning definition

Bridgepath must distinguish exposure from understanding:

- **Introduced:** the concept was explained.
- **Guided:** the child succeeded with prompts or concrete support.
- **Practised:** the child completed related examples.
- **Applied:** the child recognised and used the concept in an unfamiliar context.
- **Mastered:** independent application was observed more than once, across time or contexts.

The key test is transfer. A child who can calculate `2 + 3` has shown a procedure. A child who can recognise that operation inside an unfamiliar, worded problem and explain or demonstrate the solution has stronger evidence of understanding.

The parent loop is:

`detect a gap → open the relevant mission → teach and practise → apply independently → show evidence → suggest one parent follow-up`

## Recommended first experience

The first town will cover Standard 3 Mathematics. Its first complete adventure remains Market Day, subject to educator validation against the official curriculum.

Market Day should prove the whole product thesis:

- A community character demonstrates money, totals, payment, and change.
- The child manipulates concrete values with support.
- Practice varies the presentation rather than repeating one question form.
- The final mission uses a budget and worded context to test transfer.
- The parent sees the objective, evidence level, misconception, and one household activity.

One excellent 18-stop Arouca Grove journey and one complete evidence loop are more valuable than prematurely assigning thin content across Kairana's five future towns.

## Visual reference assessment

### Bookworm references

The useful pattern is spatial hierarchy: a world or chapter overview, distinctive destinations, a central character anchor, and clear progression into a selected area. Bridgepath should not copy Bookworm’s characters, locked-object composition, ornamental framing, or combat identity.

### Duolingo reference

The useful pattern is path legibility: one obvious current node, a visible next step, and completed versus unavailable states. Bridgepath should not import streak pressure, generic circular nodes, currencies, or a course-dashboard feeling.

### Existing Bridgepath artwork

- The village background communicates warm Caribbean community life, but it reads as a close town panorama rather than a country overview. It is better suited to the interior of a curriculum town.
- Niko and Zuri are strong recurring guides and should orient the child without becoming the only agents who solve problems.
- The destination assets share a coherent illustrated style, but the current Adventure, Customize, Mini-games, Achievements, and Parent destinations describe application modes rather than the approved curriculum-world hierarchy.
- The country overview should prioritise curriculum towns. Customisation, achievements, and parent access belong in persistent secondary navigation or appropriate in-world locations, not as equal world destinations.
- Literal Trinidad and Tobago town names will not be used. Fictional places allow cultural authenticity without ranking real communities or requiring geography to serve as curriculum levels.

## Pilot boundaries

### Included

- Parent-managed family account and one or more child profiles.
- Standard placement controlled by the parent.
- Fictional country overview with one available Standard 3 Mathematics town.
- One complete Market Day adventure with teaching, guided work, practice, remediation, independent application, and resumable progress.
- Parent view showing curriculum objective, evidence state, recent performance, support need, and suggested follow-up.
- Private child achievements tied to meaningful learning evidence.

### Excluded

- Teacher accounts and school administration.
- Claims of complete Standards 3–5 coverage.
- SEA ranking or prediction.
- Public leaderboards, chat, advertising, or behavioural marketing.
- Free-roaming open-world mechanics.
- English content in the first validated release.
- Voice-dependent learning; all instructions must work as text and interaction.

## Success criteria

The pilot is successful only if it demonstrates learning and parent usefulness:

- A child can enter the town, identify the next mission, and complete the core journey without adult navigation.
- A child who initially needs support can later solve a meaningfully different application problem independently.
- The system never labels one guided or repeated question as mastery.
- Parents can accurately explain what their child understands and name the next action after viewing the report.
- Parents return their child to the recommended mission or complete the suggested offline activity.
- Children experience the adventure as the reason to continue, while every required action still teaches, practises, or applies curriculum.

Registration, streaks, time-on-site, and reward collection are diagnostic metrics, not proof of educational success.

## Principal risks

1. **Content breadth:** promising Standards 3–5 too early could produce shallow curriculum coverage.
2. **False mastery:** attractive progress maps may overstate learning unless evidence rules remain strict.
3. **Parent anxiety:** national-readiness language could become ranking or pressure rather than useful guidance.
4. **Game-learning separation:** if children can enjoy the world while avoiding learning, the adventure becomes a distraction.
5. **Cultural authenticity:** fictional places still require review by Trinidad and Tobago children, parents, educators, writers, and illustrators.
6. **Competitive clarity:** “local and gamified” is already occupied; Bridgepath must own early gap detection plus applied understanding.
7. **Production cost:** each town requires curriculum design, writing, illustration, interaction design, evidence rules, and testing.

## Decisions made

- Parent-first pilot; schools later.
- Standards 3–5 are the long-term product boundary.
- Standard 3 Mathematics is the first content boundary.
- Each Standard-and-subject curriculum becomes its own fictional town and adventure map.
- The larger country overview makes the future world visible.
- Real Trinidad and Tobago town names will not be mapped to academic levels.
- Applied understanding, not familiar-question accuracy, is the core learning outcome.
- Product direction: Mastery Adventure Platform.

## Open decisions

- Whether future curriculum towns appear as named previews, silhouettes, or remain undisclosed.
- Which Standard 3 Mathematics unit set defines the first town’s complete release boundary.
- Parent pricing and whether the initial pilot is paid, subsidised, or free with an explicit willingness-to-pay test.
- Exact diagnostic experience before a child enters the adventure path.
- How much child choice is permitted between parallel missions without weakening prerequisite order.
- Named curriculum, cultural, privacy, and child-development reviewers.

## The assignment

Interview five parents of children currently in Standards 3 or 4. Do not demonstrate Bridgepath first. Ask them to show the latest report or describe the last school-progress conversation, explain how they discovered a learning gap, what they did next, what lessons or materials they paid for, and what evidence would make them trust an online tool’s recommendation.

Success means obtaining specific behaviour, spending, and decision evidence—not compliments about the idea.

## What I noticed

- The initial phrase “complete the curriculum” became more precise when you described the parent need: knowing exactly what the child should cover next.
- Your strongest insight was not the game concept. It was the question, “At that point is it too late, and what are we really doing—just teaching them to answer questions?”
- The Bookworm reference is doing useful product work for you: it gives you a mental model for nested worlds and curriculum progression, not merely an aesthetic to imitate.
- You protected the larger Standards 3–5 vision while accepting a one-town validation boundary. That separation between product vision and first-release scope should remain explicit.
