## Integrated experience model

Each destination uses one connected but deliberately changing arc:

1. **School arrival:** Niko, Zuri, and the learner encounter a concrete object, image, record, or problem from the town.
2. **Teacher-led meaning:** the teacher establishes what the concept means through concrete and pictorial representations before formal procedure.
3. **Classroom interaction:** the learner manipulates, predicts, classifies, measures, or explains.
4. **Guided practice:** prompts fade across parallel examples; this may record introduced, guided, or practised evidence but never independent application.
5. **Bell and transition:** a believable town need gives the class learning an immediate destination.
6. **Community encounter:** the resident explains what needs to happen and why it matters; the resident does not reteach the concept.
7. **Mechanic and mini-game:** the child practises the mathematical action through the destination's materials and activity.
8. **Independent mission:** the representation, values, or conditions change and teaching cues disappear.
9. **Support branch:** errors produce misconception-specific feedback, a smaller model, and a parallel retry without punishment.
10. **Resolution:** the community need is met, the environment changes, evidence is recorded, and the next relationship or destination is seeded.

The school segment should normally take about four to six minutes and the town application about six to nine minutes. Either side of the bell may be a safe stopping boundary. The rhythm varies by stop: not every class starts at a board, not every resident sends a message, and not every mission ends by filling a progress bar.

# Complete destination blueprint

## Stop 1 — The Grand Library Book Hunt

### Locked curriculum function

Counting and sequencing within 10,000; connecting quantities, number names, and numerals; benchmark estimation; accurate number language.

### School lesson

- **Teacher:** Main Mathematics Teacher.
- **Concept:** large quantities become manageable when organised into known groups; estimates should use a benchmark and be checked.
- **Teaching model:** a return cart of book cards is reorganised into ones, tens, hundreds, and thousands. Quantity, numeral, and number name change together on the class record.
- **Classroom interaction:** learners predict which of three collections is greatest, build a four-digit quantity, and match a physical grouping to its numeral and written name.
- **Guided practice:** estimate a smaller collection using a visible group of 100, then count by groups and explain the difference between estimate and exact count.

### Transition

- **Why Niko and Zuri leave school:** the Community Librarian has received mixed return carts and cannot reopen two reading sections until the collections are restored.
- **How the destination is introduced:** a mislabelled book crate and return slip arrive at school; after the bell, the route leads directly to the library return entrance.

### Town application

- **Destination:** The Grand Library Book Hunt.
- **Community character:** Community Librarian.
- **Character's real need:** returned books, shelf labels, and collection records do not agree.
- **Learner's role:** organise, estimate, count, label, sequence, and locate the collection whose record cannot be correct.
- **Interactive mechanic:** drag books into grouped carts; switch between quantity, numeral, and number-name views; place collection cards on an ordered shelf route.
- **Mini-game:** **Return Chute Sort**—books arrive in short untimed waves and must be routed by matching quantity representations. Accuracy and explanation matter more than speed.
- **Final application mission:** independently restore three sections, estimate before opening one cart, verify the exact count, and identify a mismatched record using a changed collection and no benchmark overlay.

### Support and remediation

- **Likely misconceptions:** counting every item without grouping; digit reversal; treating an estimate as an exact answer; reading a four-digit numeral by individual digits.
- **Feedback:** preserve the child's grouping and identify the first place where quantity and record diverge.
- **Additional support:** restore a visible group of ten or 100, model one smaller analogous cart, and reconnect objects, spoken number, written name, and numeral.
- **Retry consequence:** the section remains “being sorted” while the learner receives a parallel cart; no books or rewards are lost.

### Evidence

- **Introduced:** viewed and manipulated the linked quantity/name/numeral model.
- **Practised:** accurately grouped, sequenced, named, and estimated familiar library collections with recorded support level.
- **Applied independently:** restored a changed collection and explained a benchmark-based estimate without teaching overlays.
- **Mastered or needs support:** this stop can establish applied evidence, not mastery; later accurate use in Stops 2, 5, 6, or 16 contributes to mastery. Persistent representation or counting mismatches create a needs-support signal.
- **Parent-facing summary:** “Your child organised and represented numbers to 10,000 and used a known group to estimate. Next, Bridgepath will use place value to compare and round these quantities.”

### Character continuity

- **Previous appearances:** first town relationship.
- **Future appearances:** the Librarian's records return in the Community Radio Station and Grand Community Fair.
- **Relationship development:** the learner begins as a visitor and leaves as someone trusted to restore access to a shared community resource.

### Progression

- **Prerequisite stop:** none; assumes earlier-Standard one-to-one counting and smaller whole-number knowledge.
- **Why this stop appears here:** every later operation and measure depends on stable quantity representation.
- **Next stop connection:** correctly recorded library supply totals reveal why the position of each digit matters at the Harbour Storehouse.

## Stop 2 — The Harbour Storehouse

### Locked curriculum function

Place value to 9,999; expanded and symbolic forms; digit value; constrained largest/smallest numbers; comparison, ordering, and rounding to tens, hundreds, and thousands.

### School lesson

- **Teacher:** Main Mathematics Teacher.
- **Concept:** a digit's position determines its value; exact and rounded numbers answer different questions.
- **Teaching model:** stackable place-value crates move among four bays. Regrouping ten of one unit creates one of the next without changing the total.
- **Classroom interaction:** build shipment numbers from digit plates, state one digit's value, and compare shipments from the highest meaningful place.
- **Guided practice:** construct largest and smallest valid numbers under constraints, then round a shipment total for quick space planning while preserving the exact ledger value.

### Transition

- **Why Niko and Zuri leave school:** Mr. Thomas is waiting for building materials, but damaged harbour labels could send the wrong quantities to his project.
- **How the destination is introduced:** the teacher unfolds an exact manifest beside a rounded storage note; the difference makes sense only at the working storehouse.

### Town application

- **Destination:** The Harbour Storehouse.
- **Community character:** Mr. Thomas.
- **Character's real need:** his supplies must be identified and unloaded in the correct order while the storehouse estimates bay space for all arrivals.
- **Learner's role:** repair labels, represent quantities, compare shipments, direct unloading, and create both exact and rounded records.
- **Interactive mechanic:** regroup animated crate stacks across place-value bays and position shipments on an unloading lane.
- **Mini-game:** **Crane Bay Director**—select the correct bay from place-value clues and repair one damaged label; no speed requirement.
- **Final application mission:** independently produce the exact unloading order and rounded storage plan, then explain why one visually similar four-digit record would misdirect Mr. Thomas's supplies.

### Support and remediation

- **Likely misconceptions:** comparing by the first noticeable digit rather than highest place; confusing digit and value; always rounding up; treating rounded and exact totals as interchangeable.
- **Feedback:** highlight only the first differing place or rounding place and ask what its value represents.
- **Additional support:** return to physical bay regrouping and a three-digit parallel example before restoring four digits.
- **Retry consequence:** a crane pauses safely above the holding lane; the child revises the plan without losing a shipment.

### Evidence

- **Introduced:** connected crate groups, place positions, expanded form, and numeral.
- **Practised:** built, compared, ordered, and rounded shipments with decreasing prompts.
- **Applied independently:** made a correct exact order and a fit-for-purpose rounded plan under new digit constraints.
- **Mastered or needs support:** applied evidence is recorded; later place-value use in operations and measurement is required for mastery. Repeated positional comparison or rounding-rule errors flag support.
- **Parent-facing summary:** “Your child used place value to read, compare, order, and round four-digit quantities, and explained when an estimate was suitable.”

### Character continuity

- **Previous appearances:** Mr. Thomas is introduced as a builder receiving supplies rather than as a mathematics instructor.
- **Future appearances:** his materials reappear in the Builders' Yard and Playground Project.
- **Relationship development:** the learner sees that today's number record affects a real project later in town.

### Progression

- **Prerequisite stop:** Stop 1.
- **Why this stop appears here:** operations, estimation, conversions, and scale reading depend on positional value.
- **Next stop connection:** labelled materials include patterned kite pieces, shifting attention from individual quantities to the rule that generates a sequence.

## Stop 3 — The Kite-Maker's Yard

### Locked curriculum function

Ascending, descending, repeating, and growing patterns using operations and fractions; multiplication/division facts; compatible numbers; missing or incorrect elements; stating and applying a rule.

### School lesson

- **Teacher:** Supporting Teacher.
- **Concept:** a pattern is governed by a repeatable rule, not merely by what “looks next.”
- **Teaching model:** kite-tail pieces and number cards grow together; the class compares repeating and growing structures and tracks material totals.
- **Classroom interaction:** predict a later element, identify the first broken element, and choose which verbal rule matches a sequence.
- **Guided practice:** construct a new stage from a rule, then state a rule for a class-created pattern and test it beyond the next immediate element.

### Transition

- **Why Niko and Zuri leave school:** Auntie Joy's kite workshop has several tails assembled from conflicting pattern plans.
- **How the destination is introduced:** a colourful but visibly incorrect tail section arrives for the class to inspect; the bell releases them to test the full designs in the yard.

### Town application

- **Destination:** The Kite-Maker's Yard.
- **Community character:** Auntie Joy.
- **Character's real need:** complete kite tails and panels must follow their intended rules, and later designs require accurate material predictions.
- **Learner's role:** inspect, repair, extend, explain, and create patterns so the kites can be finished and flown.
- **Interactive mechanic:** snap pieces onto a tail or panel while a linked number sequence records material growth.
- **Mini-game:** **Catch the Broken Rule**—inspect several moving kite tails and select the first element that violates the stated rule, then repair it.
- **Final application mission:** independently finish two patterns, predict non-immediate material needs, and create a valid alternative design satisfying a new rule and limited materials.

### Support and remediation

- **Likely misconceptions:** continuing by appearance alone; confusing repeating with growing; applying the operation inconsistently; predicting only the next element without stating a general rule.
- **Feedback:** show the last confirmed transition and ask whether the same change occurs again.
- **Additional support:** reduce the sequence to three stages, physically overlay repeating units or growth additions, and model a parallel sequence.
- **Retry consequence:** the kite stays on the workbench rather than failing in flight; a new tail with the same structure but different colours provides the retry.

### Evidence

- **Introduced:** observed the link between material change, number sequence, and rule.
- **Practised:** extended, repaired, and described multiple pattern types with support recorded.
- **Applied independently:** predicted later elements and constructed a new valid design from a stated rule.
- **Mastered or needs support:** later use in visual symmetry, schedules, and multiplication contributes to mastery. Repeated copying without a valid rule signals support.
- **Parent-facing summary:** “Your child identified and explained number-pattern rules, repaired errors, and used a rule to predict later values.”

### Character continuity

- **Previous appearances:** Auntie Joy's materials arrived through the harbour supply flow.
- **Future appearances:** she returns at the Tailor's Loft, Mas Camp, and Grand Community Fair.
- **Relationship development:** she begins to treat the learner as a thoughtful maker who can explain a design, not merely decorate one.

### Progression

- **Prerequisite stop:** Stops 1–2.
- **Why this stop appears here:** recognising change and rules prepares learners to reason about operations and inverse relationships.
- **Next stop connection:** a kite parcel sent from the yard appears in the post records, where an unknown value breaks the balance.

## Stop 4 — The Parcel Post Mystery

### Locked curriculum function

Equality as balance; unknowns on either side of the equals sign; all four operations; inverse reasoning; missing quantities; verification and explanation.

### School lesson

- **Teacher:** Main Mathematics Teacher.
- **Concept:** the equals sign describes the same value on both sides; an unknown can occur anywhere and an inverse operation can recover or check it.
- **Teaching model:** two parcel shelves physically balance while parcels enter, leave, or become hidden; the corresponding number sentence changes with the model.
- **Classroom interaction:** choose which parcel group restores equality and explain why placement of the blank changes the needed reasoning.
- **Guided practice:** solve unknowns in varied positions using objects, bar models, and symbols, then verify each through an inverse operation.

### Transition

- **Why Niko and Zuri leave school:** the Postal and Delivery Coordinator has an incomplete dispatch record and one route cannot close until its parcel movement is reconciled.
- **How the destination is introduced:** a route slip with a blank arrives alongside Auntie Joy's kite parcel, linking the mystery to the previous stop.

### Town application

- **Destination:** The Parcel Post Mystery.
- **Community character:** Postal and Delivery Coordinator.
- **Character's real need:** arrival, shelf, and dispatch totals must balance so every parcel reaches the right route.
- **Learner's role:** trace parcel movements, reconstruct missing values, verify records, and locate the first incorrect entry.
- **Interactive mechanic:** move parcels among balanced shelves and rollers while number sentences update in multiple equivalent forms.
- **Mini-game:** **Balance the Route Shelves**—restore incomplete records from changing known values and inspect one plausible but incorrect solution.
- **Final application mission:** independently reconcile three connected parcel movements with unknowns in different positions and use an inverse check to identify the record that caused the mismatch.

### Support and remediation

- **Likely misconceptions:** reading equals as “the answer comes next”; applying the same operation wherever the blank appears; changing both sides inconsistently; checking by repeating the original calculation.
- **Feedback:** return the child's values to the two shelves and show whether they balance without announcing the replacement value.
- **Additional support:** use a smaller analogous parcel count, cover only one quantity, and explicitly pair an operation with its inverse before a parallel retry.
- **Retry consequence:** the affected route remains on the sorting bench; no character is blamed and no parcel is lost.

### Evidence

- **Introduced:** connected physical balance, number sentence, unknown, and inverse operation.
- **Practised:** solved varied unknown positions and checked guided examples.
- **Applied independently:** reconciled a changed multi-movement record and explained the first invalid equality.
- **Mastered or needs support:** applied evidence is recorded; later independent inverse checks in Stops 5, 6, 13, and 14 contribute to mastery. Persistent equals-sign or inverse confusion triggers support.
- **Parent-facing summary:** “Your child treated equality as balance, found missing values in different positions, and checked solutions with inverse operations.”

### Character continuity

- **Previous appearances:** Auntie Joy's labelled parcel provides a familiar object; the Coordinator's role is newly introduced.
- **Future appearances:** the Coordinator returns at the Busy Delivery Depot and contributes route records to the final fair.
- **Relationship development:** the child earns trust by correcting a system without accusing a person.

### Progression

- **Prerequisite stop:** Stops 1–3.
- **Why this stop appears here:** inverse and equality reasoning are required to check every later operation and measurement allocation.
- **Next stop connection:** the restored route includes a shop invoice whose missing price turns balance reasoning into a real money decision.

## Stop 5 — The Corner Shop Challenge

### Locked curriculum function

Addition and subtraction to four digits; up to four addends; one- and multi-step money problems; bills, best buy, profit/loss, dollars and cents; estimation, mental strategies, inverse checking, and mathematical communication.

### School lesson

- **Teacher:** Main Mathematics Teacher.
- **Concept:** addition combines quantities, subtraction finds change, difference, or a missing part; the relationship in the situation determines the operation.
- **Teaching model:** a short class supply list is represented with money, an open number line, a part-whole model, and a bill. An estimate remains visible beside the exact calculation.
- **Classroom interaction:** sort situations by mathematical relationship rather than keyword, choose a useful estimate, and complete one missing bill entry.
- **Guided practice:** calculate multi-item totals and differences, work backward from a total, compare two valid combinations, and verify using an inverse operation.

### Transition

- **Why Niko and Zuri leave school:** Mr. Ali has received the corrected invoice, but several community supply orders must be completed within their stated amounts before afternoon collection.
- **How the destination is introduced:** the teacher returns the repaired invoice to Niko and Zuri; the bell coincides with the shop opening its collection window.

### Town application

- **Destination:** The Corner Shop Challenge.
- **Community character:** Mr. Ali.
- **Character's real need:** accurate orders, bills, stock differences, and value choices are needed for real customers and community activities.
- **Learner's role:** build baskets, estimate and calculate totals, recover missing values, compare options, and explain the best valid choice.
- **Interactive mechanic:** select shelf items, compose money, edit quantities, and inspect a live bill that distinguishes estimate from exact total.
- **Mini-game:** **Receipt Repair**—match items and quantities to a damaged receipt, calculate missing lines, and flag one total that fails an inverse check.
- **Final application mission:** independently complete a multi-item order within a budget, recover a missing price or change amount, and justify which of two valid combinations better meets the stated need.

### Support and remediation

- **Likely misconceptions:** choosing an operation from a keyword; misaligning place values; ignoring cents; treating “best buy” as lowest sticker price; repeating rather than inverting a check.
- **Feedback:** identify whether the child's result is reasonable against the estimate and preserve correctly calculated bill lines.
- **Additional support:** reduce the basket, restore concrete money or a part-whole model, model one parallel relationship, and rebuild to the original number of decisions.
- **Retry consequence:** the basket is held at the counter while one line is corrected; the customer is not angry and progress is not blocked.

### Evidence

- **Introduced:** connected story relationships, models, operations, and bill notation.
- **Practised:** solved totals, differences, missing values, and value comparisons with recorded hint level.
- **Applied independently:** completed a changed multi-step purchase and justified a valid decision using estimation and an inverse check.
- **Mastered or needs support:** this is strong applied evidence but not mastery. Later independent operation use in delivery, measurement, time, statistics, and the final mission is required. Persistent place alignment, money notation, or operation-selection errors trigger support.
- **Parent-facing summary:** “Your child used addition and subtraction to plan a purchase, checked the result, and explained a value choice—not just the cheapest price.”

### Character continuity

- **Previous appearances:** the parcel invoice and harbour supplies show how goods reached the shop.
- **Future appearances:** Mr. Ali returns at the Farmers' Market and supplies the Grand Community Fair.
- **Relationship development:** he gradually gives the learner more choice, moving from checking a list to trusting a reasoned purchasing decision.

### Progression

- **Prerequisite stop:** Stops 2 and 4, with counting from Stop 1.
- **Why this stop appears here:** additive relationships and multi-step checking must be secure before equal grouping, rate, and remainder decisions.
- **Next stop connection:** completed shop orders enter the depot, where repeated quantities must be packed and routed.

## Stop 6 — The Busy Delivery Depot

### Locked curriculum function

Two-digit by two-digit multiplication; arrays and distributive property; four-digit by one-digit division; rates, unitary reasoning, contextual remainders, estimation, inverse checking, and multi-step problems.

### School lesson

- **Teacher:** Main Mathematics Teacher.
- **Concept:** multiplication and division describe equal groups, arrays, rates, and inverse relationships; a remainder must be interpreted in context.
- **Teaching model:** classroom supply packs transform among an array, decomposed area model, repeated groups, multiplication equation, and division record.
- **Classroom interaction:** build the same total with different arrays, split a factor to use the distributive property, and decide what a remainder means in three contrasting situations.
- **Guided practice:** calculate packages and trips from rates, find a unit rate, and check division through multiplication.

### Transition

- **Why Niko and Zuri leave school:** the Postal and Delivery Coordinator now has the shop orders, library supplies, and building materials, but packing and vehicle plans are incomplete.
- **How the destination is introduced:** several labelled items from earlier stops appear together on a rolling depot manifest after the bell.

### Town application

- **Destination:** The Busy Delivery Depot.
- **Community character:** Postal and Delivery Coordinator.
- **Character's real need:** goods must be grouped into complete packages and assigned to trips; leftover quantities require different decisions depending on the recipient.
- **Learner's role:** arrange, calculate, pack, route, interpret remainders, and revise when capacity changes.
- **Interactive mechanic:** reshape arrays, fill containers, allocate loads to vehicles, and connect each physical plan to multiplication/division records.
- **Mini-game:** **Pack the Route**—choose a packing arrangement, calculate complete loads, and select the correct real-world remainder action.
- **Final application mission:** independently prepare several linked deliveries, calculate a repeated rate, and revise the plan when one vehicle's capacity changes without restarting valid work.

### Support and remediation

- **Likely misconceptions:** treating multiplication as unrelated repeated addition; reversing quotient and remainder; always discarding or rounding up a remainder; applying the distributive split incorrectly; checking division with addition.
- **Feedback:** animate the child's grouping plan to expose empty or overfilled packages and compare it with the numerical record.
- **Additional support:** use a smaller array, colour the decomposed factors, reconstruct one unit rate, and offer a parallel load with the same structure.
- **Retry consequence:** affected packages remain in a safe holding bay; already correct deliveries stay packed.

### Evidence

- **Introduced:** linked arrays, equal groups, multiplication, division, rates, and remainders.
- **Practised:** decomposed factors, calculated packages and trips, and interpreted varied remainders.
- **Applied independently:** completed and revised a multi-step delivery plan using appropriate remainder decisions and inverse checking.
- **Mastered or needs support:** later fraction sharing, measurement grouping, and final planning supply transfer evidence. Persistent array/equation mismatch or context-free remainder treatment flags support.
- **Parent-facing summary:** “Your child used multiplication and division to pack and route deliveries and explained what a remainder meant in the situation.”

### Character continuity

- **Previous appearances:** the Coordinator returns from the Parcel Post Mystery with greater trust; items from Mr. Ali, the Librarian, Auntie Joy, and Mr. Thomas create continuity.
- **Future appearances:** completed deliveries feed the Bakehouse, Builders' Yard, Farmers' Market, and final fair.
- **Relationship development:** the learner moves from correcting one record to coordinating a small working system.

### Progression

- **Prerequisite stop:** Stops 3–5.
- **Why this stop appears here:** equal grouping and division create the conceptual bridge to equal fractional parts.
- **Next stop connection:** one delivery contains trays and ingredients that must be shared fairly at the Bakehouse.

## Stop 7 — The Big Bakehouse

### Locked curriculum function

Fractions through area, set, and linear models; equal parts; numerator and denominator; words and symbols; introductory equivalence; connection to division and equality.

### School lesson

- **Teacher:** Main Mathematics Teacher.
- **Concept:** a fraction describes equal parts in relation to a defined whole; numerator and denominator have relational meanings.
- **Teaching model:** the same fraction is represented with a tray area, a set of counters, and a measured strip. Unequal partitions are contrasted explicitly.
- **Classroom interaction:** identify the whole, create fair partitions, match three representations, and explain why an attractive but unequal model is invalid.
- **Guided practice:** reconstruct wholes, name fractions in words and symbols, and use sharing/division to create equivalent-looking amounts in different models.

### Transition

- **Why Niko and Zuri leave school:** Miss Maria has received ingredients and trays from the depot, but several group orders require fair portions and matching preparation records.
- **How the destination is introduced:** a covered tray with an obviously unequal partition is brought to class; after the bell the learners go to the working Bakehouse to correct the complete order.

### Town application

- **Destination:** The Big Bakehouse.
- **Community character:** Miss Maria.
- **Character's real need:** trays, sets of items, and measured ingredient strips must be divided fairly among different groups.
- **Learner's role:** define the whole, partition equally, label each share, translate among models, and repair unfair preparation plans.
- **Interactive mechanic:** cut or group virtual materials along allowable boundaries and watch area, set, linear, word, and symbol representations stay linked.
- **Mini-game:** **Fair Share Builder**—prepare changing group orders and identify the one arrangement that looks similar but does not preserve equal parts.
- **Final application mission:** independently prepare three group orders using different model types and produce matching records without relying on one familiar food cue.

### Support and remediation

- **Likely misconceptions:** counting parts without checking equality; changing the whole; treating denominator as the shaded amount; believing more pieces always means more quantity.
- **Feedback:** outline the whole and superimpose equal-size comparisons without replacing the child's partition immediately.
- **Additional support:** return to physical sharing for two groups, then move through area, set, and linear models with one parallel fraction.
- **Retry consequence:** the tray remains at the preparation table and is repartitioned; food is not portrayed as wasted.

### Evidence

- **Introduced:** connected whole, equal parts, numerator, denominator, and three model types.
- **Practised:** built, named, matched, and repaired fraction representations with varying support.
- **Applied independently:** prepared fair shares across changed wholes and models and explained why an invalid partition failed.
- **Mastered or needs support:** independent transfer at the Tailor's Loft and later measurement contexts is required for mastery. Unstable whole/equal-part reasoning flags support.
- **Parent-facing summary:** “Your child represented fractions as equal parts of areas, sets, and lengths and explained what the numerator and denominator describe.”

### Character continuity

- **Previous appearances:** her supplies arrived through the Corner Shop and Delivery Depot.
- **Future appearances:** Miss Maria contributes measured preparation needs to the Farmers' Market and final fair.
- **Relationship development:** she recognises the learner's care with fairness and later trusts them with more complex measures.

### Progression

- **Prerequisite stop:** Stop 6, with equality from Stop 4.
- **Why this stop appears here:** fraction meaning must be stable before equivalence, comparison, mixed numbers, or operations.
- **Next stop connection:** Bakehouse cloth covers and measured ribbons move the same fractional ideas from food/sharing into length and material.

## Stop 8 — The Tailor's Loft

### Locked curriculum function

Equivalent fractions; comparing and ordering proper fractions with unlike denominators; number lines; proper, improper, and mixed forms; conversions; adding and subtracting like-denominator fractions.

### School lesson

- **Teacher:** Supporting Teacher.
- **Concept:** a fractional amount can be renamed without changing its value; fractions are numbers that can be compared, positioned, combined, and extend beyond one whole.
- **Teaching model:** transparent fraction strips subdivide while maintaining length, then align to a number line that crosses one whole.
- **Classroom interaction:** prove equivalence, position values, compare unlike denominators without denominator shortcuts, and build one mixed/improper pair.
- **Guided practice:** exchange equal-length strips, order several lengths, convert forms, and add/subtract like-denominator pieces while preserving the unit.

### Transition

- **Why Niko and Zuri leave school:** Auntie Joy needs measured fabric, ribbon, and cord pieces prepared for several town orders, but the available pieces use different fractional labels.
- **How the destination is introduced:** an offcut bundle contains two differently labelled pieces that are visibly the same length; the bell sends the learners to test substitutions on the full measuring table.

### Town application

- **Destination:** The Tailor's Loft.
- **Community character:** Auntie Joy.
- **Character's real need:** material targets must be assembled from available pieces, including equivalent substitutions and quantities beyond one whole.
- **Learner's role:** measure, exchange, order, combine, convert, and justify material choices.
- **Interactive mechanic:** place fabric and cord strips on a shared measuring rail and number line; cut only along equal fractional divisions.
- **Mini-game:** **Fabric Exchange**—replace unavailable pieces with equivalent combinations and reject substitutions that only look similar.

- **Mini-game:** **Evidence or Opinion?**—inspect short town claims and their graphs, repair misleading representations, and identify what can responsibly be reported.
- **Final application mission:** independently produce an accurate report from the Mangrove Count and one other town dataset, calculate a decision threshold, and recommend an action supported by the evidence.

### Support and remediation

- **Likely misconceptions:** treating each bar block as one regardless of scale; omitting axes/labels; copying frequencies into bar height inaccurately; reading visual height without scale; inferring a claim not measured.
- **Feedback:** connect the selected bar to its source row and count scale intervals rather than revealing the answer.
- **Additional support:** rebuild one small graph at scale 1, then transform the same data to scale 2 or 5 before a parallel report.
- **Retry consequence:** the segment remains in editing; no incorrect information is broadcast and no public embarrassment occurs.

### Evidence

- **Introduced:** connected source frequencies, graph parts, scale, calculations, claims, and decisions.
- **Practised:** constructed, transformed, interpreted, and repaired multiple representations with support recorded.
- **Applied independently:** created a changed scaled graph and justified a recommendation from its evidence.
- **Mastered or needs support:** final-fair data use provides additional transfer; mastery also depends on reliable inquiry evidence from Stop 16. Persistent scale, label, transformation, or unsupported-claim errors flag support.
- **Parent-facing summary:** “Your child built and interpreted scaled bar graphs and checked that a community recommendation was supported by the data.”

### Character continuity

- **Previous appearances:** the Librarian returns from Stop 1, now trusting the learner with public information rather than book records; datasets refer to familiar people and places.
- **Future appearances:** the report informs the Grand Community Fair and appears at earlier destinations.
- **Relationship development:** the learner's responsibility grows from making records accurate to communicating their meaning responsibly.

### Progression

- **Prerequisite stop:** Stop 16 plus skip counting, place value, operations, and measurement-scale experience.
- **Why this stop appears here:** it completes the statistics progression and gives the learner evidence for integrated town planning.
- **Next stop connection:** the report identifies attendance, supply, space, or schedule needs that must be coordinated at the final fair.

## Stop 18 — The Grand Community Fair

### Locked curriculum function

Independent integration across N1–N13, G1–G4, M1–M7, and S1–S4 using accumulated longitudinal evidence. The mission samples selected objectives and cannot alone establish mastery of the entire Standard.

### School lesson

- **Teacher:** Main Mathematics Teacher and Supporting Teacher together.

- **Classroom interaction:** analyse one small unfamiliar planning need, identify relevant/irrelevant information, and compare two defensible strategies.
- **Guided practice:** strategic prompts only—clarify, represent, choose, estimate, solve, check, and explain. Previously taught models remain available on request but are not preselected.

- First feedback preserves valid work and identifies the conceptual mismatch
- Repeated error reduces simultaneous decisions and restores a concrete model
- Smaller analogous demonstration followed by a parallel, non-identical retry
- Continued difficulty offers review/safe stop and records a neutral support signal
- Reversible story consequence only; no hearts, shame, permanent loss, or blocked instruction

## F. Evidence and resolution

- Introduced/guided/practised/applied event recorded separately
- Objective, context, content version, support level, attempt, and time attached
- Mastery derived only from multiple independent observations across contexts/time
- Plain-language parent summary, formal mapping, and one useful next action
- World change or keepsake separated from academic status
- Next destination seeded through a person, object, route, or consequence

## Variation requirement

Every chapter must vary at least three of these: school opener, representation, learner action, transition device, destination mechanic, mission structure, explanation mode, environmental resolution. Reusing the evidence contract is desirable; reusing the same dramatic rhythm eighteen times is not.

# Strongest first vertical slice

## Recommendation: The Corner Shop Challenge

The Corner Shop Challenge remains the strongest product-validation slice because it demonstrates Bridgepath's complete promise in a context immediately understandable to children and parents:

- a short Storypath Primary School lesson establishes addition/subtraction relationships, estimation, money representation, and inverse checking;
- Mr. Ali has a believable community need rather than becoming a teacher;
- the learner has agency through basket and value choices;
- the mission supports concrete, pictorial, symbolic, and worded forms;
- repeated errors have clear misconception-specific remediation;
- a changed multi-step purchase produces meaningful independent evidence;
- the parent summary is easy to understand; and
- the shop visibly changes when the order and record are resolved.

The slice must use a curriculum-validated subset of Stop 5 rather than attempt every addition, subtraction, bill, best-buy, profit/loss, and money objective at once. Recommended initial evidence scope: multi-item totals, budget/difference or change, estimation, and one inverse check. Profit/loss should wait unless the signed content brief shows it can fit without overloading the adventure.

Because Stop 5 normally depends on earlier place-value and equality work, the prototype must either recruit learners with those prerequisites, include a short non-labelling readiness check, or treat missing prerequisite knowledge as support evidence. It must not claim to validate the entire path sequence from a standalone slice.

# Special review findings

1. **School length:** each lesson has one conceptual model, one active interaction, and short guided transfer. Any stop that requires multiple class sessions should become resumable learning stops without altering town order.
2. **Structural variety:** contexts alternate among searching, directing, making, investigating, purchasing, packing, sharing, designing, travelling, planning, measuring, scheduling, observing, reporting, and coordinating.
3. **Resident integrity:** teachers teach; residents state needs, constraints, consequences, and occupational checks. No resident introduces a rule the child has not encountered at school.
4. **Mathematical necessity:** every mission fails its story purpose if its mathematics is removed. Decorative calculations should be cut during content review.
5. **Caribbean authenticity:** community life is carried through institutions, skilled work, transport, recreation, ecology, materials, and relationships—not through constant Carnival, food, accent, or steelpan shorthand.
6. **Explanation:** independent missions require the child to select, build, order, match, or briefly state why an answer works. Voice capture is unnecessary; accessible choice, sequencing, and construction alternatives remain valid.
7. **Final mission:** the fair is a constraint-coordination performance with multiple valid plans and a changed condition, not a quiz reel or one aggregate score.
