## Status

Proposed educational blueprint for review. This document defines the learning destinations and sequence inside the still-unnamed Standard 3 Mathematics town.

All stop names are functional working titles. They do not approve town geography, final place names, characters, dialogue, stories, artwork, or implementation.

The blueprint is derived from:

- the supplied Standard 3 Mathematics Curriculum Guide;
- the supplied 2024 TTNLA Standard 3 Mathematics paper; and
- `STANDARD-3-MATHEMATICS-EXPERIENCE-CLUSTERS.md`.

The guide determines the objectives and conceptual dependencies. The past paper reveals transfer demands but does not determine the learning experience.

## Journey design

The complete journey contains 18 primary learning stops. A stop is a meaningful destination where mathematics is needed for a recognisable activity. It is not a lesson container.

The path is sequential for navigation but spiral in learning:

- each stop introduces a primary concept family;
- earlier concepts return in new contexts;
- mathematical language, estimation, mental strategies, explanation, and checking recur throughout;
- application missions require the child to choose the mathematics rather than follow a named procedure; and
- later stops combine evidence from earlier destinations.

No destination owns a curriculum objective permanently. The first appearance establishes meaning; later appearances provide varied practice, application, and mastery evidence.

# Phase 1 - Quantities become usable

## Stop 1 - The Community Register

### Why this stop exists

Every later activity depends on understanding that spoken numbers, written numerals, number names, and collections represent the same quantities. This stop establishes large-number meaning before formal procedures begin.

### Curriculum objectives covered

- N1: count sequentially within 10,000, including forward, backward, and skip counting;
- connect number names and numerals with quantities;
- read, write, and sequence number names and numerals;
- N2: estimate collections using a known benchmark such as 100 and verify the estimate;
- N13: use accurate number language.

Guide reference: Number, pages 5-6. TTNLA transfer signals: items 1 and 4 require secure representation and four-digit reasoning.

### Prerequisite concepts

- one-to-one counting;
- number sequence and place-value awareness from earlier Standards;
- grouping objects into manageable sets;
- reading and writing smaller whole numbers.

### Authentic Caribbean context

A community, school, library, sports club, garden group, or family event must record people, books, plants, equipment, or supplies accurately. Children participate by counting, estimating, grouping, labelling, and checking rather than completing an abstract list.

### Type of mentor

A records keeper, librarian, school secretary, club organiser, or grower who relies on trustworthy counts.

### Learning experience

The learner encounters collections at different scales, chooses efficient grouping strategies, connects each collection to number words and numerals, sequences records, and compares an estimate with a verified count.

### Interactive demonstration

A collection is reorganised into groups of tens and hundreds while the visible numeral and number name update. A benchmark group remains visible so the mentor can model estimating before exact counting.

### Guided practice

The child groups partially organised collections, completes missing labels, places records in sequence, and improves an estimate after comparing it with a benchmark. Support reduces visual density and provides pre-grouped units.

### Reusable mini-game opportunities

- estimate, organise, verify;
- match collection, numeral, and number name;
- repair an incorrectly sequenced register;
- count forward or backward through changing quantities;
- find the record that cannot match its collection.

### Application mission

Prepare a reliable registration or inventory record for several categories, including one collection too large to count one item at a time. The learner must estimate, choose a grouping strategy, record exact quantities, and correct one inconsistent entry.

### Evidence of understanding

- preserves quantity across objects, words, and numerals;
- counts and sequences accurately within 10,000;
- uses a benchmark-based estimate rather than a random guess;
- explains the grouping strategy;
- detects a mismatch between record and collection.

### Why this stop comes before the next one

The learner first needs quantities to be meaningful and representable. The next stop examines how the position of each digit carries value and makes comparison, ordering, and rounding possible.

## Stop 2 - The Supply Ledger

### Why this stop exists

Large records are useful only when children understand place value. This stop turns four-digit numerals into structured quantities that can be decomposed, compared, ordered, and approximated.

### Curriculum objectives covered

- N3: represent place value to 9,999 using concrete, pictorial, expanded, and symbolic forms;
- state the value of a digit by position;
- build largest and smallest valid four-digit numbers under constraints;
- N4: compare and order numerals to 10,000 using greater than, less than, and equal to;
- N5: round to the nearest ten, hundred, and thousand;
- N2 and N10: estimate and use place-value mental strategies;
- N13: use place-value and comparison language.

Guide reference: Number, pages 7-8. TTNLA transfer signals: items 1, 2, and 4.

### Prerequisite concepts

- Stop 1 quantity/number/word connections;
- grouping by tens and hundreds;
- sequencing whole numbers.

### Authentic Caribbean context

A storeroom, produce depot, library, cooperative, or school office must interpret and compare stock, attendance, or supply records. Rounding supports quick planning before an exact decision is required.

### Type of mentor

An inventory keeper, depot coordinator, librarian, or cooperative organiser who moves between exact records and useful approximations.

### Learning experience

The learner builds ledger quantities from grouped objects, decomposes them into expanded notation, compares records place by place, orders deliveries, and rounds values for planning.

### Interactive demonstration

Place-value columns receive crates, bundles, or counters. Exchanging ten units for one ten, ten tens for one hundred, and ten hundreds for one thousand changes the representation without changing the quantity. A comparison tool highlights the first place where two numerals differ.

### Guided practice

The learner completes missing expanded forms, identifies a digit's value, constructs numbers from digit cards under conditions, compares records, and positions values between rounding benchmarks. Support restores grouped objects or a number line.

### Reusable mini-game opportunities

- build, decompose, and rename a number;
- arrange digits under largest/smallest and odd/even constraints;
- compare two records and identify the decisive place;
- place a number between rounding landmarks;
- repair an incorrectly expanded ledger entry.

### Application mission

Organise several supply records from least to greatest, produce exact and rounded totals for two different planning needs, and resolve a discrepancy caused by a digit entered in the wrong place.

### Evidence of understanding

- connects grouped quantity, numeral, and expanded form;
- states digit value accurately;
- compares from the highest relevant place rather than by visual guess;
- rounds according to the requested place;
- explains why an approximation is suitable or insufficient.

### Why this stop comes before the next one

Place value provides a stable number structure. The next stop asks children to detect change within sequences and state the rule producing it, preparing them to reason about operations rather than only individual answers.

## Stop 3 - The Pattern Yard

### Why this stop exists

Patterns help learners see operations as relationships that repeat predictably. This supports multiplication, division, mental mathematics, unknowns, and later visual pattern and symmetry work.

### Curriculum objectives covered

- N6: describe and extend ascending and descending number patterns using the four operations and fractions;
- recall and use multiplication and division facts through 10 times tables;
- identify compatible numbers within 1,000;
- recognise incorrect or missing elements;
- state the rule governing a pattern;
- N10: explain mental strategies;
- early transfer to G2-G3 through visual arrangements without formally introducing those objectives yet.

Guide reference: Number, pages 9-10. TTNLA transfer signals: items 12 and 34 distinguish repeating and growing patterns.

### Prerequisite concepts

- Stop 1 sequencing;
- Stop 2 place value and comparison;
- basic operation facts from earlier learning.

### Authentic Caribbean context

Patterns occur in garden rows, tiled or paved surfaces, fabric, decorative borders, seating, packing arrangements, music or movement sequences, and repeated schedules. The mathematics is the rule controlling the arrangement, not the decoration itself.

### Type of mentor

A gardener, tailor, craft worker, groundskeeper, packer, or activity organiser who needs repetition to be accurate and efficient.

### Learning experience

The learner observes numerical and visual sequences, describes what changes and what stays constant, identifies the operation or fractional step, locates errors, and predicts or constructs later elements.

### Interactive demonstration

A rule machine changes a starting quantity one step at a time while objects or markers form the corresponding arrangement. Changing the rule makes the pattern grow, shrink, repeat, or alternate.

### Guided practice

The learner fills missing terms, chooses among plausible rules, finds a deliberately misplaced element, and creates an arrangement matching a stated rule. Support exposes the change between adjacent terms.

### Reusable mini-game opportunities

- continue a route, row, border, or packing sequence;
- find and replace the incorrect element;
- match pattern to rule;
- build the fifth or later element of a growing pattern;
- create two different patterns from the same materials.

### Application mission

Complete a repeated or growing community arrangement with limited materials, find one section built under the wrong rule, and provide the quantities needed for the next two sections.

### Evidence of understanding

- states a valid rule in words and/or symbols;
- distinguishes repeating from growing structure;
- predicts non-immediate elements through reasoning;
- recognises errors rather than copying them;
- constructs a new valid element.

### Why this stop comes before the next one

Pattern reasoning makes change and inverse relationships visible. The next stop formalises equivalence, missing values, and operations that undo one another.

## Stop 4 - The Balance Desk

### Why this stop exists

Children often treat the equals sign as an instruction to calculate rather than a statement that two expressions have the same value. This stop establishes equality and inverse reasoning before multi-step commerce and operation problems.

### Curriculum objectives covered

- N7: solve number sentences containing unknowns on either side of the equals sign using all four operations;
- use inverse operations and explain reasoning;
- N8-N9: connect operation meanings to missing quantities;
- N10: use mental and place-value strategies;
- N13: communicate equality, difference, total, factor, product, quotient, and remainder accurately.

Guide reference: Number, page 10 with later operation elaborations on pages 11-17. TTNLA transfer signals: items 17, 18, 24, 29, and 31.

### Prerequisite concepts

- Stops 1-2 number meaning and place value;
- Stop 3 operation rules and missing elements;
- basic addition, subtraction, multiplication, and division facts.

### Authentic Caribbean context

Records, scales, packages, bills, stock movements, teams, and construction materials create missing-quantity problems. A balance may be physical, numerical, or relational.

### Type of mentor

A records clerk, scale operator, storekeeper, builder, or packer who must reconcile two sides of a record or physical arrangement.

### Learning experience

The learner builds equal quantities, changes one side while preserving balance, identifies missing values in different positions, and uses inverse operations to recover or check a quantity.

### Interactive demonstration

A physical balance and a number sentence update together. Adding, removing, grouping, or ungrouping on one side shows why a corresponding relationship must hold on the other.

### Guided practice

The child fills unknowns using objects, diagrams, and symbols; determines which inverse operation can verify the result; and diagnoses examples where a shown procedure produces a remainder or total that does not match the original relationship.

### Reusable mini-game opportunities

- balance two physical or numeric sides;
- recover missing stock from before-and-after records;
- repair a broken number sentence;
- choose the inverse check;
- decide whether shown working is valid and identify the first error.

### Application mission

Reconcile a set of community records containing missing initial quantities, changed stock, and one incorrect calculation. The child must restore equivalence and justify at least one correction.

### Evidence of understanding

- interprets equality as balance;
- solves unknowns in varied positions;
- chooses an operation based on the relationship;
- verifies through an inverse operation;
- explains why an answer satisfies both sides.

### Why this stop comes before the next one

The learner now understands how operations relate and can recover missing values. The next destination applies addition and subtraction to totals, differences, bills, and value decisions without reducing the work to a memorised word cue.

## Stop 5 - The Community Supply Counter

### Why this stop exists

Addition and subtraction become strategically meaningful when a child must combine, compare, revise, or work backward from real quantities. Money adds constraints that require estimation and reasonableness, not merely computation.

### Curriculum objectives covered

- N8: add values up to four digits with up to four addends;
- subtract values up to four digits;
- solve one- and multi-step real-life problems, including money;
- interpret bills, best buy, profit and loss, and dollars and cents;
- use acting out, drawings, tables, organised lists, work backward, logical reasoning, and algorithms;
- estimate and check using inverse operations;
- create and interpret number stories;
- N10 and N13: mental strategies and mathematical communication.

Guide reference: Number, pages 11-13. TTNLA transfer signals: items 15, 17, 18, and 19.

### Prerequisite concepts

- place value and rounding from Stop 2;
- equality and inverse reasoning from Stop 4;
- addition and subtraction facts.

### Authentic Caribbean context

A household, school, club, cooperative, shop, or community group must prepare a supply list, compare available options, check a bill, and remain within a budget. A market or shop is one valid setting among several.

### Type of mentor

A vendor, cooperative organiser, household planner, school purchasing clerk, or community volunteer.

### Learning experience

The learner estimates totals, calculates exact amounts, combines several costs, finds differences, works backward from a known total, distinguishes cost from selling price, and compares choices.

### Interactive demonstration

An open number line, place-value money model, and item table show several valid ways to combine or separate amounts. Estimation appears before the exact calculation and remains visible for a reasonableness check.

### Guided practice

The learner completes missing bill entries, selects an operation for different change structures, compares combinations satisfying a target, and checks totals through inverse reasoning. Support returns to objects, a number line, or a simpler table.

### Reusable mini-game opportunities

- build and check a bill;
- find two or more combinations meeting a total;
- compare purchase and sale values;
- locate an incorrect receipt line;
- choose an option under quantity and budget constraints.

### Application mission

Prepare a complete supply purchase from several options, remain within a stated budget, recover one missing price or quantity, and determine whether a later sale or exchange produces a profit or loss.

### Evidence of understanding

- chooses addition or subtraction from the relationship;
- coordinates multiple pieces of information;
- estimates before calculating;
- verifies through inverse operations;
- distinguishes value terms correctly;
- justifies a valid choice.

### Why this stop comes before the next one

Addition and subtraction establish change and comparison. The next stop extends this reasoning to equal groups, repeated quantities, division, rates, and contextual remainders.

## Stop 6 - The Packing and Delivery Yard

### Why this stop exists

Multiplication and division are most understandable as relationships among totals, equal groups, arrays, rates, and remainders. Packing and delivery make those relationships visible and consequential.

### Curriculum objectives covered

- N9: multiply up to two-digit by two-digit values;
- use area/array models and the distributive property;
- divide up to four-digit values by one digit;
- solve one- and multi-step real-life problems, including money and rates;
- apply unitary reasoning;
- interpret remainders according to context;
- estimate and check using inverse operations;
- N10: mental multiplication/division strategies;
- N13: explain procedures and solutions.

Guide reference: Number, pages 14-18. TTNLA transfer signals: items 3, 5, 20, 29, 30, 31, and 32.

### Prerequisite concepts

- Stops 1-3 counting, place value, skip counting, and patterns;
- Stop 4 inverse relationships;
- Stop 5 multi-step addition/subtraction and estimation.

### Authentic Caribbean context

Goods, produce, books, sports kits, seedlings, food, or event supplies must be arranged in equal packages, loaded, delivered at a rate, and redistributed when totals do not divide evenly.

### Type of mentor

A depot worker, delivery coordinator, grower, coach, food producer, or transport organiser.

### Learning experience

The learner builds arrays, decomposes factors, connects repeated groups to multiplication, interprets division as sharing and measuring, calculates rates, and decides what a remainder means for people, packages, vehicles, or leftover resources.

### Interactive demonstration

Objects move between an array, equal packages, a multiplication expression, and a division expression. Splitting a factor shows the distributive property. Changing package size exposes quotient and remainder.

### Guided practice

The child completes arrays, selects efficient decompositions, fills packages, calculates quantities from per-unit rates, and chooses an appropriate response to remainders. Support provides smaller arrays, factor facts, or a visual grouping tray.

### Reusable mini-game opportunities

- pack equal groups under changing constraints;
- build an array from a product and find alternatives;
- split a difficult multiplication into friendly parts;
- complete a delivery rate table;
- decide whether to keep, share, round up, or create another package for a remainder;
- inspect and explain a division error.

### Application mission

Prepare several types of supplies for delivery: calculate total production, create equal packages, determine vehicle or trip needs from a rate, and handle one remainder in a way that makes sense for the situation.

### Evidence of understanding

- connects array, grouping, multiplication, and division;
- uses the distributive property meaningfully;
- solves multi-step quantity/rate problems;
- interprets remainders contextually;
- checks division through multiplication;
- explains a chosen strategy.

### Why this stop comes before the next one

Equal grouping and division provide the conceptual foundation for equal parts. The next stop turns fair sharing into fraction meaning across wholes, sets, and lengths.

# Phase 2 - Parts and forms

## Stop 7 - The Sharing Table

### Why this stop exists

Fractions must begin with equal partitioning and relationships, not with memorising numerator and denominator. A sharing context makes fairness visible while multiple models prevent one food image from becoming the concept.

### Curriculum objectives covered

- N11: recognise and represent fractions using area, set, and linear models;
- name and record fractions in words and symbols;
- explain numerator and denominator;
- identify equivalent representations of the same fractional amount at an introductory level;
- N7 and N9: equality, division, and fair grouping connections;
- N13: precise fraction language.

Guide reference: Number, page 19. TTNLA transfer signals: items 6 and 16.

### Prerequisite concepts

- equal groups and division from Stop 6;
- equality from Stop 4;
- patterns and counting;
- understanding of a whole and equal-sized parts from earlier Standards.

### Authentic Caribbean context

Family or community food, fruit, trays, material bundles, garden plots, and route lengths may need to be divided or described fairly. Food is an entry context, not the only representation.

### Type of mentor

A cook, baker, gardener, family organiser, craft worker, or community volunteer who shares resources fairly.

### Learning experience

The learner establishes the whole, partitions it into equal parts, names selected parts, and shows the same fraction as part of an area, a set, and a line. Unequal partitions are used to expose misconceptions.

### Interactive demonstration

One amount changes among a tray, basket of items, and marked strip or route while numerator and denominator remain linked to selected parts and total equal parts.

### Guided practice

The child partitions wholes and sets, labels fractions, distinguishes equal from merely numerous parts, and matches equivalent-looking quantities across model types. Support overlays equal partitions or returns to physical grouping.

### Reusable mini-game opportunities

- share a whole or set fairly;
- find the partition that cannot represent the named fraction;
- match area, set, line, word, and symbol;
- reconstruct a whole from a fractional part;
- adjust a sharing plan when the number of recipients changes.

### Application mission

Prepare fair shares of several different resources and produce a record showing each share as an area, set, and linear fraction. One proposed sharing plan is unequal and must be corrected and explained.

### Evidence of understanding

- identifies the whole consistently;
- creates equal parts;
- explains numerator and denominator relationally;
- represents the same fraction in three model types;
- detects an unfair or invalid partition;
- connects sharing to division.

### Why this stop comes before the next one

The learner needs stable fraction meaning before comparing, renaming, combining, or working beyond one whole. The next stop deliberately removes the initial sharing cue and transfers fractions to lengths, materials, measures, and number lines.

## Stop 8 - The Fabric and Measure Table

### Why this stop exists

Understanding fractions requires seeing relationships among differently named quantities and quantities greater than one. Linear materials and measured pieces support equivalence, comparison, mixed forms, and operations without depending on circular food models.

### Curriculum objectives covered

- N12: recognise and generate equivalent fractions;
- compare and order proper fractions with unlike denominators using equivalents and number lines;
- distinguish proper fractions, improper fractions, and mixed numbers;
- convert between improper and mixed forms;
- add and subtract fractions with the same denominator using models and mental reasoning;
- N11 and N13: maintain model meaning and accurate language;
- early transfer to M1, M4-M6 through fractional measures.

Guide reference: Number, pages 19-20. TTNLA transfer signals: items 7, 16, 21, and 33.

### Prerequisite concepts

- Stop 7 fraction meaning across three models;
- multiplication/division and equality;
- number lines, comparison, and whole-number ordering.

### Authentic Caribbean context

Fabric, ribbon, rope, garden rows, route segments, measured ingredients, time intervals, and repeated containers require comparing and combining fractional lengths or units.

### Type of mentor

A tailor, craft worker, gardener, cook, builder, or route planner who measures parts precisely and combines them into wholes.

### Learning experience

The learner lays fractional strips on the same whole, finds equivalence, orders unlike denominators through common reference points or equivalents, assembles more than one whole, and combines or removes like-denominator parts.

### Interactive demonstration

Strips stretch across a fixed whole or number line. Subdividing a strip changes its name but not its length. Joining pieces crosses the one-whole boundary and updates proper, improper, and mixed notation.

### Guided practice

The child builds equivalent pairs, orders fractions on a line, converts assembled pieces between forms, and solves same-denominator addition/subtraction using visible lengths before symbols. Support restores overlays and benchmark points such as zero, one-half, and one.

### Reusable mini-game opportunities

- cover the same length with differently divided pieces;
- place fractions on a route or measuring line;
- build a requested mixed amount;
- exchange improper and mixed labels;
- combine or remove like-sized parts to meet a material requirement.

### Application mission

Prepare measured materials for several needs, compare competing fractional lengths, substitute equivalent pieces when one size is unavailable, and combine parts to produce an amount greater than one whole.

### Evidence of understanding

- proves equivalence with a model;
- compares unlike denominators without using denominator size as a false shortcut;
- positions fractions on a number line;
- converts between improper and mixed forms meaningfully;
- adds/subtracts like-denominator fractions while preserving the unit.

### Why this stop comes before the next one

Fractions have now connected quantity with area, sets, and length. The next stop shifts to the properties of two- and three-dimensional forms that make objects and structures usable.

## Stop 9 - The Workshop

### Why this stop exists

Geometry becomes meaningful when shapes and solids must be recognised by their properties, represented, and constructed. The workshop makes orientation, faces, edges, vertices, and polygon properties functional.

### Curriculum objectives covered

- G1: identify and describe cubes, cuboids, cylinders, pyramids, cones, triangular prisms, and spheres;
- distinguish regular and irregular solids where applicable;
- identify faces, edges, and vertices;
- build frames, sketch solids, and solve problems from properties;
- G2: identify and classify triangles, quadrilaterals, pentagons, hexagons, and octagons;
- recognise regular and irregular polygons in varied orientations;
- G4: use precise geometry language;
- N9 and N12 transfer: count components and use fractional/whole relationships in materials.

Guide reference: Geometry, pages 22-25. TTNLA transfer signals: items 11 and 25.

### Prerequisite concepts

- counting and classification;
- patterns and comparison;
- fraction and linear-model awareness;
- informal shape knowledge from earlier Standards.

### Authentic Caribbean context

Boxes, containers, roofs, frames, signs, paving, furniture, stalls, home repairs, school projects, and craft use solids and polygons because particular properties serve particular purposes.

### Type of mentor

A carpenter, builder, repairer, craft worker, package maker, or school project facilitator.

### Learning experience

The learner handles, rotates, classifies, sketches, and constructs shapes and solid frames; identifies components; and selects a form for a stated purpose rather than by superficial resemblance.

### Interactive demonstration

A solid can be rotated, made transparent, reduced to an edge frame, or compared with its plane faces. A polygon remains classified correctly while its orientation and proportions change.

### Guided practice

The child sorts objects by properties, counts and labels faces/edges/vertices, builds frames from different-length components, recognises rotated polygons, and explains why a form belongs in a class. Support highlights one property at a time.

### Reusable mini-game opportunities

- classify by property rather than appearance;
- construct a frame with the correct components;
- identify a hidden solid from property clues;
- rotate and match plane shapes;
- select suitable forms for packing, rolling, stacking, or building constraints.

### Application mission

Prepare a set of functional containers, frames, or signs for different uses, choosing forms by properties and calculating the required component types. One proposed structure must be diagnosed and repaired.

### Evidence of understanding

- identifies forms from defining properties;
- remains accurate under rotation;
- counts faces, edges, and vertices;
- distinguishes solids from their plane faces;
- constructs or sketches a valid example;
- explains why a form suits a requirement.

### Why this stop comes before the next one

The learner first needs shape properties and orientation invariance. The next stop uses those forms to reason about reflection, symmetry, and deliberate visual structure.

## Stop 10 - The Sign and Pattern Studio

### Why this stop exists

Symmetry and pattern become purposeful in signs, fabric, borders, decoration, craft, and clear public design. This stop revisits numerical patterns through spatial structure and requires children to create, not only recognise.

### Curriculum objectives covered

- G3: determine whether a shape or design is symmetrical;
- identify the number and position of lines of symmetry;
- examine symmetry in letters and numerals where appropriate;
- complete and create symmetrical shapes;
- G2 and G4: use polygon properties and geometry language;
- N6: revisit repeating and growing rules through visual arrangements.

Guide reference: Geometry, pages 23-25 and Number patterns pages 9-10. TTNLA transfer signals: items 12, 26, and 34.

### Prerequisite concepts

- Stop 3 rule and pattern reasoning;
- Stop 9 plane-shape properties and orientation;
- grid position and equal distance awareness.

### Authentic Caribbean context

Community signs, fabric, craft, garden arrangements, painted borders, school displays, and preparations for ordinary gatherings may use symmetry and repeated motifs. Cultural patterns require review and should not be copied as decoration without context.

### Type of mentor

A sign painter, tailor, visual craft worker, gardener, or community display organiser.

### Learning experience

The learner tests designs for symmetry, predicts reflected positions, completes missing halves, identifies lines of symmetry, and applies a stated pattern rule across a design.

### Interactive demonstration

A virtual fold or mirror shows corresponding points at equal distance from a line. Pattern controls reveal which features repeat, reflect, rotate, or grow.

### Guided practice

The child places reflected points on grids, completes figures, tests letters and shapes, corrects an almost-symmetrical design, and extends borders under a rule. Support shows fold lines and corresponding point pairs.

### Reusable mini-game opportunities

- complete a reflected sign or motif;
- find the incorrect mirrored point;
- choose all valid lines of symmetry;
- extend a border while preserving its rule;
- create different symmetrical designs from the same components.

### Application mission

Finish a set of clear, balanced signs or decorations by applying symmetry and pattern rules, repair one design with a subtle error, and produce an original valid arrangement under material limits.

### Evidence of understanding

- predicts reflected position accurately;
- identifies valid symmetry lines;
- distinguishes symmetry from mere similarity;
- states and applies a pattern rule;
- creates rather than only selects a valid example;
- explains the correction made.

### Why this stop comes before the next one

Making and design create a genuine need for precise dimensions. The next stop introduces formal length and distance units so plans can move from visual fit to reliable measurement.

# Phase 3 - Measure and coordinate

## Stop 11 - The Route Marker Station

### Why this stop exists

Length and distance require understanding the attribute, choosing a sensible unit, estimating, measuring, and converting. Route and marker contexts make centimetres, metres, and kilometres distinguishable by scale.

### Curriculum objectives covered

- M1: measure and compare using kilometres, metres, and centimetres;
- understand and use relationships among metric units;
- choose an appropriate unit;
- estimate length or distance and verify;
- convert within the specified units;
- solve real-life problems involving Number and money;
- M7: use precise measurement language;
- N5, N8-N10: rounding, operations, and mental strategies.

Guide reference: Measurement, pages 26-27. TTNLA transfer signal: item 32 exposes repeated-distance and interval reasoning.

### Prerequisite concepts

- place value and multiplication/division;
- fractions on a linear model;
- geometry and spatial orientation;
- estimation habits.

### Authentic Caribbean context

Walking routes, roads, delivery stops, school grounds, gardens, fabric, repair materials, playing spaces, and household dimensions require different units and degrees of precision.

### Type of mentor

A route or delivery coordinator, groundskeeper, gardener, builder, tailor, or family repairer.

### Learning experience

The learner estimates, selects units and tools, measures, converts, compares routes or materials, and counts intervals correctly when repeated markers define a distance.

### Interactive demonstration

The same length is iterated with centimetre and metre units while a wider map view introduces kilometres. Objects and intervals are separately highlighted to show why ten markers create nine spaces.

### Guided practice

The child chooses units, positions measuring tools from zero, estimates before revealing exact measures, converts related values, and solves repeated-interval problems. Support provides unit benchmarks and marks endpoints.

### Reusable mini-game opportunities

- choose the sensible unit and tool;
- estimate then measure;
- convert a route or material record;
- place markers at equal intervals;
- find the distance from first to last marker;
- compare two routes under distance constraints.

### Application mission

Prepare a route or material plan using centimetres, metres, and kilometres appropriately, place repeated markers correctly, and revise the plan when one distance changes.

### Evidence of understanding

- selects units by scale and precision;
- measures from a valid starting point;
- estimates reasonably;
- converts accurately;
- distinguishes number of points from number of intervals;
- explains a route or material choice.

### Why this stop comes before the next one

Perimeter is composed of lengths, and area requires reliable linear units and arrays. The next stop applies measurement to boundaries and surfaces.

## Stop 12 - The Grounds Plan

### Why this stop exists

Children must distinguish distance around a boundary from surface coverage. Planning gardens, play spaces, paths, or community areas makes perimeter and area different practical needs.

### Curriculum objectives covered

- M2: calculate and compare perimeter of regular and irregular shapes;
- distinguish perimeter from area;
- solve real-life perimeter problems involving Number and money;
- M6: estimate, compare, and calculate area using square centimetres and square metres;
- select an appropriate square unit;
- calculate area on a grid and understand conservation of area;
- G2 and N9: use shape properties, arrays, multiplication, and factors;
- M7: communicate attribute and unit accurately.

Guide reference: Measurement, pages 27-28 and 34-36. TTNLA transfer signals: items 9, 22, and 33.

### Prerequisite concepts

- Stop 9 plane shapes;
- Stop 11 linear measurement and units;
- multiplication arrays and factors;
- fraction area models.

### Authentic Caribbean context

Garden beds, yards, playing spaces, floor or wall surfaces, fences, drains, paths, fabric, and pavilion areas require either boundary material, surface material, or both.

### Type of mentor

A gardener, builder, groundskeeper, painter, tiler, tailor, or community-space planner.

### Learning experience

The learner traces boundaries, covers surfaces with square units, builds shapes of specified area or perimeter, compares designs, and discovers that equal area does not guarantee equal perimeter.

### Interactive demonstration

A grid-based shape can be rearranged while preserving its square-unit area. Boundary length updates separately, making conservation and distinction visible.

### Guided practice

The child calculates regular and irregular perimeters, counts and multiplies square units, creates rectangles from factor pairs, selects units, and compares alternative designs. Support highlights boundary segments or overlays unit squares.

### Reusable mini-game opportunities

- trace boundary versus cover surface;
- build a requested area in more than one way;
- find designs with equal area but different perimeter;
- estimate material before exact calculation;
- fit a usable space within area and boundary limits.

### Application mission

Design a garden, play, display, or work space under separate surface and boundary constraints, calculate the required materials, and revise the design when one dimension changes.

### Evidence of understanding

- chooses perimeter or area for the stated need;
- uses linear and square units correctly;
- calculates irregular boundaries;
- constructs a valid area on a grid;
- demonstrates conservation through rearrangement;
- explains trade-offs between designs.

### Why this stop comes before the next one

The learner has distinguished and measured spatial attributes. The next stop introduces mass as a different measurable attribute, reinforcing that the unit and tool must match what is being measured.

## Stop 13 - The Produce Scale

### Why this stop exists

Mass is often confused with object size or count. Weighing produce and packages establishes mass as an attribute that can be estimated, measured, compared, converted, and used in quantity decisions.

### Curriculum objectives covered

- M3: estimate, measure, compare, and order mass using grams and kilograms;
- understand 1,000 g = 1 kg;
- convert between grams and kilograms;
- read scales and packaging information;
- solve real-life problems involving Number and money;
- N4-N5, N8-N10: compare, round, calculate, group, and check;
- M7: use mass language and notation accurately.

Guide reference: Measurement, pages 29-30. TTNLA transfer signals: items 7 and 24.

### Prerequisite concepts

- place value and 1,000 relationships;
- multiplication/division and equality;
- comparison and estimation;
- fraction meaning for partial kilograms.

### Authentic Caribbean context

Produce, ingredients, parcels, groceries, animal feed, garden materials, and household packages are sold, prepared, or distributed by mass.

### Type of mentor

A produce vendor, grower, cook, shopkeeper, package handler, or cooperative organiser.

### Learning experience

The learner estimates before weighing, reads different scale positions, compares objects with misleading visual sizes, converts records, balances repeated masses, and coordinates mass with quantity or price.

### Interactive demonstration

A balance and dial scale respond to objects while gram and kilogram records update. Equal masses with different shapes expose the difference between mass, size, and count.

### Guided practice

The child selects g or kg, reads scales, assembles target masses, converts, orders packages, and uses repeated unit masses to balance an unknown. Support provides benchmark objects and subdivided scales.

### Reusable mini-game opportunities

- estimate then weigh;
- build a target mass from packages;
- balance repeated unit masses;
- match package label to scale reading;
- compare price and mass under a stated need.

### Application mission

Prepare and label several weighed packages, substitute available masses to reach targets, and resolve one mismatch between a written label and the scale.

### Evidence of understanding

- distinguishes mass from visual size;
- selects g or kg sensibly;
- reads scale intervals;
- converts between units;
- composes and compares masses;
- uses balance/equality reasoning to explain a correction.

### Why this stop comes before the next one

Mass establishes a metric unit relationship and scale-reading habit. Capacity uses a parallel but distinct attribute, helping children choose between how heavy something is and how much a container can hold.

## Stop 14 - The Water and Container Station

### Why this stop exists

Capacity requires reasoning about container amount, not mass or appearance. Water, drinks, food preparation, gardening, and storage make millilitres, litres, conversions, and fractional units visible.

### Curriculum objectives covered

- M5: estimate, measure, compare, and order capacity using millilitres and litres;
- use halves, quarters, and multiples of units;
- understand and convert litre/millilitre relationships;
- solve real-life problems involving Number and money;
- N11-N12: apply fractions to measured quantities;
- N8-N10: use operations and grouping;
- M7: use capacity language accurately.

Guide reference: Measurement, pages 32-33. TTNLA transfer signals: items 10 and 23.

### Prerequisite concepts

- place value and 1,000 relationships;
- multiplication/division;
- fraction meaning and equivalence;
- estimation and metric-unit experience from Stop 13.

### Authentic Caribbean context

Drinking water, juice, ingredients, watering plants, household containers, food stalls, and community preparation require measuring and allocating liquid capacity.

### Type of mentor

A cook, food vendor, gardener, household caregiver, shopkeeper, or water/resource coordinator.

### Learning experience

The learner estimates containers, fills and pours measured amounts, compares shapes with equal capacity, converts records, determines how many smaller containers fit, and adapts a plan when container sizes change.

### Interactive demonstration

Liquid transfers among differently shaped containers while a calibrated jug and symbolic record preserve the amount. One litre is partitioned into halves, quarters, and millilitres.

### Guided practice

The child chooses mL or L, assembles target capacities, converts, divides a large amount among smaller containers, and identifies misleading shape-based judgments. Support adds calibration marks and unit benchmarks.

### Reusable mini-game opportunities

- estimate then fill;
- compare capacity despite container shape;
- compose one litre in several ways;
- convert and relabel containers;
- determine the number of complete smaller containers;
- revise a distribution plan after a container change.

### Application mission

Prepare sufficient liquid for several uses, select containers, fill exact amounts, convert the records, and adjust the plan after one container becomes unavailable without wasting the supply.

### Evidence of understanding

- distinguishes capacity from mass and height;
- chooses mL or L appropriately;
- connects fractional litres with millilitres;
- converts and divides accurately;
- determines complete containers and handles any remainder meaningfully;
- explains why the allocation is sufficient.

### Why this stop comes before the next one

Capacity reinforces unit relationships and fractional quantities. The next stop applies similar part-whole and operation reasoning to hours, minutes, duration, dates, and schedules.

## Stop 15 - The Schedule Board

### Why this stop exists

Time is not only clock reading. Children must coordinate representations, intervals, duration, a.m./p.m., 24-hour time, calendars, and dates to plan real activity.

### Curriculum objectives covered

- M4: tell time in five-minute intervals on analogue and digital clocks;
- use a.m. and p.m.;
- interpret and use 24-hour time;
- calculate duration;
- read and use calendars;
- write and interpret dates in supported formats;
- solve time problems;
- N8-N12: use operations and fractions of an hour;
- M7 and N13: communicate time precisely.

Guide reference: Measurement, pages 30-32. TTNLA transfer signals: items 8 and 21.

### Prerequisite concepts

- skip counting by five;
- addition/subtraction and work-backward reasoning;
- fraction understanding of half and quarter;
- sequence and interval reasoning;
- number notation.

### Authentic Caribbean context

School and family schedules, maxi-taxi or delivery times, sports training, appointments, opening hours, food preparation, clubs, and community activities require coordination.

### Type of mentor

A dispatcher, driver, coach, school coordinator, cook, event organiser, or household planner.

### Learning experience

The learner translates between time systems, positions events, calculates elapsed time forward and backward, uses calendars, identifies conflicts, and revises a schedule after a delay or changed duration.

### Interactive demonstration

An analogue clock, digital display, 24-hour record, and timeline move together. Duration appears as an interval rather than a subtraction trick. A calendar connects date sequence with scheduled activity.

### Guided practice

The child sets and reads times, labels a.m./p.m., converts selected 24-hour times, calculates duration using timelines, works backward from a start, and repairs schedule conflicts. Support uses five-minute arcs and segmented timelines.

### Evidence of understanding

- uses a.m./p.m. and 24-hour notation correctly;
- calculates duration forward and backward;
- interprets fractions of an hour;
- uses calendar/date information;
- explains the impact of a schedule change.

### Why this stop comes before the next one

The learner can now record quantities, measures, and times across many contexts. The next stop asks how to turn those observations into an answerable question and a reliable dataset.

# Phase 4 - Evidence and integration

## Stop 16 - The Community Question Point

### Why this stop exists

Statistics begins with a real question, not a finished graph. Children need to understand what data should be collected, how categories are defined, and how tallies preserve observations.

### Curriculum objectives covered

- S1: formulate a real-life problem or question;
- decide what data is relevant;
- collect, classify, and organise observations;
- S2: record and read tally marks and frequency information;
- S4: communicate method and early findings;
- N1, N4, N8, N13: count, compare, total, and label accurately.

Guide reference: Statistics, pages 37-39. TTNLA transfer signal: item 13.

### Prerequisite concepts

- Stop 1 counting and records;
- clear categories and number language;
- comparison and addition;
- experience with authentic variables from Stops 5-15.

### Authentic Caribbean context

A family, class, club, garden group, cooperative, recreation organiser, or community project wants evidence about attendance, preferences, supplies, sales, travel, rainfall, yield, or activity use.

### Type of mentor

A librarian, school coordinator, community reporter, cooperative secretary, environmental volunteer, or recreation organiser.

### Learning experience

The learner distinguishes answerable questions from vague ones, defines categories, gathers or receives observations, classifies consistently, tallies in groups of five, totals frequencies, and identifies data-quality problems.

### Interactive demonstration

Individual observations move into categories and tally groups. Changing a category definition reveals how inconsistent classification changes the result.

### Guided practice

The child selects relevant data for a question, classifies ambiguous examples, records tallies, totals categories, and repairs a dataset containing duplicate, missing, or inconsistent entries. Support narrows the categories and models tally grouping.

### Reusable mini-game opportunities

- choose the answerable question;
- sort observations into clear categories;
- record rapid observations as tallies;
- reconcile raw observations and frequency totals;
- find a data-quality problem;
- predict what additional information is needed.

### Application mission

Conduct or complete a small investigation that will inform a real decision, producing a clear question, category plan, tally record, and verified frequency table.

### Evidence of understanding

- asks an answerable question;
- identifies relevant observations;
- classifies consistently;
- records and totals tallies accurately;
- detects data-quality issues;
- explains how the collection method connects to the question.

### Why this stop comes before the next one

A graph is only meaningful when the underlying data and categories are trustworthy. The next stop transforms verified frequencies into scaled bar graphs and uses them to reason and decide.

## Stop 17 - The Evidence Board

### Why this stop exists

Children must understand that a graph represents data according to a scale and that interpretation may require operations, thresholds, and justification. This stop connects representation to community decisions.

### Curriculum objectives covered

- S3: construct and read bar graphs;
- label axes and categories;
- choose and use scales such as 1, 2, 5, and 10;
- S4: transform among tally, pictorial, block, tabular, and bar representations;
- interpret, compare, combine, and communicate values;
- make and justify decisions from data;
- N4-N5, N8-N10, N13: compare, round, calculate totals/differences, and explain.

Guide reference: Statistics, pages 39-41. TTNLA transfer signals: items 14, 27, 28, and 35.

### Prerequisite concepts

- Stop 16 inquiry, classification, tallies, and frequencies;
- place value, skip counting, operations, comparison, and estimation;
- measurement-scale awareness.

### Authentic Caribbean context

Attendance, sales, garden yield, rainfall, transport use, sports participation, supplies, and preferences are represented so families, schools, clubs, or community groups can act on evidence.

### Type of mentor

A community information keeper, librarian, school coordinator, reporter, cooperative organiser, or environmental volunteer.

### Learning experience

The learner selects a useful scale, builds and labels a graph, translates another representation into bars, combines or compares categories, calculates what change would alter an outcome, and gives an evidence-supported recommendation.

### Interactive demonstration

The same frequency table is displayed under different valid scales. Bar height, axis intervals, and labels update together, while an intentionally misleading scale or missing label demonstrates how representation can fail.

### Guided practice

The child completes axes and bars, selects scales, transforms pictorial/tally data, answers exact and comparative questions, combines categories, and finds the smallest change required to reach a threshold. Support restores a frequency table and highlights scale intervals.

### Reusable mini-game opportunities

- choose and apply a graph scale;
- complete a graph from another representation;
- identify a misleading or incomplete graph;
- combine categories to match a target;
- determine the change needed to alter the result;
- choose and justify an action from the evidence.

### Application mission

Turn the investigation from Stop 16 into a clear evidence display, answer several decision questions requiring comparison or operations, and recommend an action while stating what the data does and does not support.

### Evidence of understanding

- preserves frequency across representations;
- chooses and applies a scale correctly;
- labels axes and categories;
- performs totals, differences, combinations, and threshold reasoning;
- distinguishes evidence from unsupported claim;
- communicates a justified decision.

### Why this stop comes before the next one

The learner can now use every major curriculum strand and can turn observations into evidence. The final destination requires independent selection and coordination of those ideas under changing conditions.

## Stop 18 - The Community Planning Table

### Why this stop exists

The complete journey needs a destination where no one tells the learner which mathematical topic to use. This stop tests whether the child can understand a need, select representations and operations, coordinate constraints, check results, and explain decisions.

### Curriculum objectives covered

Integrated application across:

- N1-N13: quantity, place value, estimation, patterns, equality, four operations, mental strategies, rates, remainders, money, and fractions;
- G1-G4: solids, polygons, orientation, symmetry, construction, and language;
- M1-M7: length, perimeter, mass, time, capacity, area, conversions, estimation, and language;
- S1-S4: investigation, tallies, graphs, scales, interpretation, decisions, and communication.

The mission samples selected objectives independently. Full curriculum status also uses accumulated evidence from Stops 1-17; one final performance cannot establish mastery of every objective.

### Prerequisite concepts

- applied evidence from every earlier stop;
- ability to read a multi-part need, choose a strategy, and persist through revision;
- familiarity with checking, explaining, and interpreting results.

### Authentic Caribbean context

A family, school, club, cooperative, or community group must prepare a shared project, service, space, or occasion. The exact context remains open for cultural and child review. It must genuinely require quantities, resources, measurement, timing, and evidence rather than attaching unrelated questions to a celebration.

### Type of mentor

A community coordinator who asks for a plan and evidence but does not demonstrate each solution. Earlier mentors may provide information from their own areas without becoming answer prompts.

### Learning experience

The learner reviews a need, identifies relevant information, separates knowns from unknowns, chooses operations and representations, creates a workable plan, checks constraints, responds to one changed condition, and communicates the reasoning.

### Interactive demonstration

No new concept is demonstrated. An accessible planning workspace shows how to organise information, inspect available tools, record assumptions, and check constraints without recommending which mathematics to use.

### Guided practice

Guidance is metacognitive rather than procedural: identify the goal, choose relevant information, represent the problem, estimate, solve, check, and revise. Optional support can reopen an earlier model or practice stop without completing the mission automatically.

### Reusable mini-game opportunities

The final stop composes previously established mechanics rather than adding a disconnected game:

- estimate, organise, verify;
- compare under constraints;
- group/share and interpret remainders;
- plan, fit, build, and test;
- route and reschedule;
- represent evidence and justify a decision.

### Application mission

Produce a complete plan that includes:

- interpreting attendance, preference, or resource data;
- determining quantities and groupings;
- comparing a constrained purchase or allocation;
- measuring or designing a usable object or space;
- coordinating a time or delivery sequence;
- adapting to one changed price, quantity, time, or availability condition; and
- explaining and checking at least two consequential decisions.

### Evidence of understanding

- independently identifies relevant concepts;
- selects suitable strategies and representations;
- coordinates multiple steps and units;
- estimates and checks reasonableness;
- adapts rather than restarts when a condition changes;
- justifies decisions with mathematical evidence;
- distinguishes completed task requirements from unsupported assumptions.

### Why this stop comes before the next one

There is no new Standard 3 destination after this one. This is the synthesis point. Its evidence joins the complete longitudinal record to show what the learner has encountered, practised, applied independently, or still needs help with. It should lead to reflection and targeted return paths, not a single pass/fail verdict.

# Path rules for later design

1. A destination must show why the mathematics exists before presenting symbols or procedures.
2. Demonstration, guided use, and independent application must use related but non-identical examples.
3. A memorable cue must fade before independent evidence is recorded.
4. An objective must reappear in at least one different context before mastery is possible.
5. Mini-games may practise a recurring mathematical action, but cannot replace application evidence.
6. Incorrect answers open explanation and support; they do not remove learning access.
7. Mental mathematics, vocabulary, estimation, and checking are recurring habits, not isolated destinations.
8. The final mission samples transfer; it does not overwrite the longitudinal evidence record.
9. The child sees destinations and community needs. Parents may expand the formal curriculum mapping behind every stop.
10. Working titles and contexts remain subject to educator, child, and cultural review before story or visual design.
