# Bridgepath Curriculum Application Framework

## Purpose

Bridgepath will not turn the curriculum into worksheets placed on a painted map. It will translate each mathematical concept into a meaningful community problem while preserving the formal Trinidad and Tobago curriculum mapping for parents and reviewers.

The child experiences a story. The system records curriculum evidence.

## The learning promise

> First understand what the mathematics means. Then use it with help. Finally carry it into a new situation.

Every substantial concept follows this sequence:

1. **Encounter:** A resident has a believable problem that creates a reason to learn.
2. **Meaning:** The learner sees what the idea represents before being given a rule.
3. **Model:** Concrete objects, pictures, diagrams, language, and symbols are connected.
4. **Guided use:** The learner acts with prompts, manipulatives, and explanatory feedback.
5. **Varied practice:** The representation or context changes so success does not depend on memorising one screen.
6. **Independent application:** The learner solves a story problem without the teaching cue.
7. **Explain and check:** The learner selects, builds, or states why an answer makes sense.
8. **Transfer:** The idea returns later in another chapter, location, or combined mission.

## The memorable-model rule

Memorable models are welcome, but the model is not the concept.

- A cake, roti, fruit tray, or pizza can introduce fractions, but learners must also work with sets and number lines.
- A hungry animal's open mouth can help children remember the direction of `>` and `<`, but learners must first compare quantities and place values, and later compare without the character cue.
- Coins and notes can make place value tangible, but understanding must transfer to ordinary numerals and other quantities.
- Arrays can reveal multiplication, but learners must later recognise multiplication in rates, groups, area, and multi-step problems.

For every mnemonic or story model:

1. establish the mathematical meaning;
2. introduce the memory cue;
3. connect the cue to symbols and language;
4. fade the cue;
5. vary the context; and
6. ask for independent reasoning.

The product must never mark a concept as mastered because a child memorised a character animation or one interaction pattern.

## Evidence ladder

| State | What happened | What it does not prove |
|---|---|---|
| Introduced | The child encountered the idea and completed the teaching interaction | Independent understanding |
| Guided | The child succeeded with prompts, models, or hints | Recall without support |
| Practised | The child succeeded across several examples or representations | Transfer to a real problem |
| Applied | The child independently used the concept inside a meaningful mission | Stable mastery over time |
| Mastered | The child applied the concept independently on multiple occasions, with varied contexts and adequate recency | Permanent or universal understanding |

Parent reporting should show the evidence state, recency, support used, and next useful action. Accuracy alone is insufficient.

## Story-to-curriculum contract

Every chapter requires two linked briefs.

### Curriculum brief

- curriculum edition and source page;
- Standard, subject, strand, content area, and objective IDs;
- prerequisite knowledge;
- precise concept meaning;
- representations learners must connect;
- likely misconceptions;
- vocabulary and symbols;
- required independent evidence; and
- educator approval.

### Adventure brief

- resident and community need;
- why the mathematics is necessary;
- visible objects that model the concept;
- teaching scene;
- guided activity;
- practice variation;
- independent mission;
- support branch;
- explanation/check moment;
- later transfer opportunity; and
- environmental change produced by success.

If the mathematics can be removed without changing the story, the story is decoration and must be revised. If the story obscures the mathematical meaning, it must also be revised.

## Naming rules

Child-facing names describe places, people, needs, and discoveries. Parent-facing views retain formal curriculum language.

| Avoid on the child map | Prefer in the world | Parent mapping |
|---|---|---|
| Unit 1: Number Concepts | The Counted Crates | Number: number concepts to 10,000 |
| Place Value Lesson | Signs and Stock | Number: place value, comparison, ordering, and rounding |
| Fractions Topic | Fair Shares at the Bakery | Number: fractions using area, linear, and set models |
| Measurement Module | Measure and Make | Measurement: length, perimeter, and area |
| Statistics Unit | Ask the Village | Statistics: tally charts, bar graphs, interpretation, and decisions |

These adventure names are provisional. Final names should be reviewed for clarity, age fit, cultural naturalness, and continuity with the complete 18-stop Arouca Groove journey.

## Assessment design implications

The 2024 TTNLA assessment moves between representations, direct calculation, contextual problems, and written reasoning. Bridgepath should prepare learners for that transfer without becoming an SEA revision bank.

Each checkpoint should therefore sample a balanced mix of:

- recognising and representing the concept;
- performing an appropriate procedure;
- choosing a strategy;
- applying the concept in a worded or visual situation;
- checking whether the result is reasonable; and
- explaining or justifying a decision.

Original questions and situations must be used. Assessment items from the reference PDF must not be copied or lightly reskinned.

## Content review standard

No chapter is content-complete until a qualified local educator confirms:

- the objective mapping is exact;
- the teaching sequence is mathematically sound;
- the models do not create misconceptions;
- the language is appropriate for Standard 3;
- the independent mission genuinely demonstrates the objective; and
- the support path teaches rather than simply makes the answer easier to guess.
